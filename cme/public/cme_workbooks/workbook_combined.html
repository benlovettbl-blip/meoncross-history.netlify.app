1. Why did Jewish demands clash with British policy in 1945?

Following the Allied victory in 1945, Britain faced a mounting crisis in Palestine. The Holocaust galvanized global support for Zionism—the movement advocating for a sovereign Jewish homeland. In the US, President Harry Truman pressured British PM Clement Attlee to immediately grant entry certificates for 100,000 Jewish refugees. Meanwhile, Palestinian Arabs, led by the Grand Mufti Haj Amin al-Husseini, vehemently opposed this, demanding immediate independence and warning of rebellion. Caught in the middle, British Foreign Secretary Ernest Bevin restricted immigration to a quota of 1,500 per month to protect relations with Arab oil-producing nations, fueling deep anger among Jewish communities. The British were effectively caught between their desperate need for Arab oil to rebuild their shattered post-war economy, and intense moral and political pressure from the US to help the Jewish refugees.

2. How did Zionist paramilitaries force the British out?

Furious at British restrictions, Jewish paramilitary organizations (Haganah, Irgun, and Lehi) launched a coordinated armed insurgency. On 22 July 1946, the Irgun bombed the British military administrative headquarters at the King David Hotel in Jerusalem, killing 91 people. The crisis escalated in July 1947 with the Sergeants Affair: the Irgun kidnapped and hanged two British sergeants (Clifford Martin and Mervyn Paice) in retaliation for British executions. This caused immense public outrage in Britain, sparked anti-Semitic riots, and destroyed domestic willingness to maintain the Mandate. The British public, exhausted from World War II and facing severe economic rationing at home, increasingly viewed the Palestine Mandate as a massive drain on blood and treasure.

3. Why did UN Resolution 181 fail to prevent war?

In July 1947, British authorities intercepted the SS Exodus carrying 4,500 Holocaust survivors and returned them to Europe, causing global media outrage. Near bankruptcy and unable to maintain order, Britain referred Palestine's future to the United Nations. On 29 November 1947, the UN passed Resolution 181 (the Partition Plan), proposing to divide Palestine into separate Jewish and Arab states, with Jerusalem under international control. Jewish leaders accepted the plan for sovereignty, but Arab leaders rejected it as unjust, since the Jewish state got 55% of the land despite Jews representing only 33% of the population. Palestinian Arabs felt their right to self-determination was completely ignored by western powers, triggering civil war. During this escalating civil war, Zionist paramilitaries (Irgun and Lehi) attacked the Arab village of Deir Yassin on 9 April 1948, killing over 100 residents, which spread widespread panic and accelerated the flight of Palestinian civilians.

When the Mandate expired on 14 May 1948, David Ben-Gurion declared the State of Israel. British forces withdrew, and five neighboring Arab armies (Egypt, Transjordan, Syria, Lebanon, Iraq) invaded. Israel won due to Arab disunity (lack of coordinated command); military consolidation (paramilitaries merged into the IDF); and a crucial ceasefire that allowed Israel to negotiate a secret arms deal with Czechoslovakia, doubling active troops from 35,000 to 108,000. By 1949, Israel captured 50% more land than originally allocated, creating a refugee crisis for 700,000 Palestinians, known as the Nakba.

Historical Interpretations: Contrasting Views

Interpretation 1: Michael J. Cohen (Traditionalist / Pragmatic Imperial Focus)

"The British decision to abandon the Mandate was the inevitable result of an exhausted empire facing an insoluble conflict. Bankrupt after World War II and unable to police 100,000 troops in Palestine, Britain realized that maintaining control was economically and politically unsustainable, especially as the conflicting demands of Arabs and Jews reached a complete deadlock."

Interpretation 2: William Roger Louis (Revisionist / Insurgency Focus)

"It was the violent Zionist insurgency, rather than abstract imperial decline, that drove Britain out. The Irgun's campaign of terror—climaxing in the devastating bombing of the King David Hotel in 1946 and the retaliatory hanging of British sergeants—destroyed British public morale, humiliated the military command, and made the Mandate politically untenable."

Synthesis Tip: When writing about the end of the Mandate, consider whether British withdrawal was pushed by external pressures (Zionist insurgency) or pulled by internal decline (post-war financial crisis).

1. How did the 1948-49 war transform the map of Palestine?

The armistice agreements of 1949 redrew the map of the Middle East. The proposed independent Palestinian Arab state was aborted. Israel captured 79% of mandate Palestine (50% more than the UN partition plan had allocated), gaining control of vast new territories including the fertile Galilee region, the Negev Desert, and West Jerusalem. Transjordan occupied the West Bank and East Jerusalem, annexing it in 1951, while Egypt took military control of the Gaza Strip. These armistice lines became known as the Green Line, consolidating Israel's victories and entrenching border divisions.

2. What caused the Palestinian refugee crisis?

The war was a disaster for Palestinian Arabs, who refer to the events of 1948 as the Nakba ('The Catastrophe'). Over 700,000 Palestinians fled or were forcibly expelled from their homes in the territory that became Israel. Fleeing in panic, they ended up in squalid, overcrowded refugee camps in Gaza, the West Bank, Lebanon, Syria, and Jordan. Israel denied them the Right of Return to prevent the dilution of its Jewish majority, creating permanent exile. In December 1949, the UN established the UNRWA (Relief and Works Agency) to manage these camps and provide basic food, healthcare, and schooling.

3. How did Israel secure its new state?

To defend the new state, Prime Minister David Ben-Gurion merged rival pre-war Jewish militias (Haganah, Irgun, and Lehi) into a single national army: the Israeli Defence Forces (IDF). Israel introduced universal conscription to secure its borders. In July 1950, Israel passed the Law of Return, granting every Jew in the world the right to settle in Israel and receive immediate citizenship. This doubled Israel's population in three years, welcoming Holocaust survivors and Jews fleeing Arab lands. This massive influx caused extreme economic strain, forcing a strict food rationing system called Tzena and heavy reliance on US aid (grants and loans totaling $300 million) and German reparations.

4. Why did tensions between Israel and Egypt remain high?

Following the war, relations between Israel and Egypt were characterized by deep hostility. Egypt resented Israel for its military defeat and for the burden of 200,000 Palestinian refugees trapped in the Egyptian-controlled Gaza Strip. Displaced Palestinians, known as the Fedayeen, launched frequent guerrilla raids from Gaza into Israel. The IDF responded with disproportionate military reprisals, escalating border tensions. Egypt also waged economic war by blockading the Suez Canal to Israeli shipping and closing the Straits of Tiran, choking Israel's southern port of Eilat.

Historical Interpretations: Contrasting Views

Interpretation 1: Efraim Karsh (Traditionalist Israeli Interpretation)

"The flight of Palestinian Arabs in 1948 was largely voluntary, driven by the collapse of local leadership and explicit instructions from Arab commanders. They urged the population to leave temporarily to clear the path for the invading Arab armies, promising a swift return once the Jewish state was destroyed. There was no pre-conceived Zionist plan to expel them."

Interpretation 2: Benny Morris (Revisionist 'New Historian' Interpretation)

"The Palestinian refugee crisis was born of war, not of design. While there was no systematic, pre-planned Zionist master plan for expulsion, the flight was primarily triggered by direct military actions of the Haganah/IDF, fear of incoming clashes, psychological warfare, and localized, deliberate expulsions by commanders on the ground, such as at Lydda and Ramle."

Synthesis Tip: GCSE questions on the aftermath of the 1948 war require you to analyze the refugee crisis. Contrast these views to show that historians debate whether displacement was a deliberate policy or a chaotic byproduct of war.

1. How did Nasser threaten Western interests in the Middle East?

Gamal Abdel Nasser came to power in Egypt in 1954, championing Pan-Arabism—the unification of Arab states to resist Western influence and oppose Israel. To modernize his army and bypass Western restrictions, Nasser signed the Czech Arms Deal in September 1955, importing modern Soviet jet fighters and tanks. This deal fundamentally shifted the regional balance of power. He also planned to construct the massive Aswan High Dam to boost Egypt's economy.

2. Why did Britain, France, and Israel form a secret alliance?

When the US and UK withdrew their funding for the Aswan Dam in July 1956, Nasser retaliated by nationalising the British-and-French-owned Suez Canal to fund the project. Outraged, Britain and France signed the secret Protocol of Sèvres with Israel. The conspiracy was simple: Israel would invade Egypt, and Britain and France would intervene as 'peacekeepers' to seize control of the canal zone.

3. How did the Suez Crisis unfold militarily?

On 29 October 1956, Israel launched a swift invasion of the Sinai Peninsula, routing Egyptian forces. As planned, Britain and France ordered both sides to withdraw from the canal, and then bombed Egyptian airfields and landed troops at Port Said. While the invasion was a military success, capturing Sinai and the canal, it was a massive political disaster.

4. Why did the superpowers force an end to the crisis?

Angry at the unilateral action, US President Dwight Eisenhower threatened to destroy the British pound by selling US sterling reserves and blocking IMF loans. The USSR threatened rocket strikes. Under this intense pressure, Britain, France, and Israel withdrew. A UN peacekeeping force (UNEF) was deployed to Sinai. Nasser emerged as a Pan-Arab hero, leading to the peak of Pan-Arabism in 1958 with the UAR (United Arab Republic), a political union between Egypt and Syria.

Historical Interpretations: Contrasting Views

Interpretation 1: Jean-Edward Smith (Traditional Western / Treaty-Based Focus)

"Nasser's nationalisation of the Suez Canal Company was a unilateral act of aggression that violated international trust and threatened the free passage of global shipping. By seizing an asset built by Western capital and vital for European oil imports, Egypt forced Britain and France to intervene to protect their legal rights and the international rules-based order."

Interpretation 2: Fawaz Gerges (Post-Colonial / Arab Nationalist Focus)

"The nationalisation was a legitimate assertion of Egyptian national sovereignty and a blow against imperialist exploitation. The Canal was dug on Egyptian soil by Egyptian peasants, yet its profits flowed exclusively to foreign shareholders. Nasser's action was a bold, necessary step to finance national development and resist Western colonial dictates."

Synthesis Tip: The Suez Crisis is a classic study of conflicting perspectives. Explain how Western historians focus on international trade laws and treaty violations, while Arab historians highlight national sovereignty and anti-colonialism.

1. How did water and the PLO increase tensions?

Tensions rose at the Cairo Conference in 1964, which established the PLO (Palestine Liberation Organization) to coordinate Palestinian resistance. Arab League states also agreed to divert the headwaters of the Jordan River to deny water to Israel's National Water Carrier, sparking frequent border skirmishes.

2. Why did border skirmishes escalate into crisis?

Syria actively backed Arafat's Fatah group, allowing guerrilla raids into northern Israel. The IDF responded with harsh reprisals, including the Samu Raid in Jordan in November 1966. Tensions escalated on 7 April 1967 when Israeli jets shot down six Syrian MiG fighters over Damascus during border artillery duels.

3. What events triggered the Six-Day War?

In May 1967, the USSR falsely warned Egypt that Israel was massing troops to invade Syria. Nasser expelled UN peacekeepers (UNEF) from Sinai, deployed 100,000 troops, and closed the Straits of Tiran to Israeli shipping, blockading the port of Eilat. Jordan signed a mutual defense pact with Egypt, creating an encirclement crisis.

4. How did Israel achieve such a rapid victory?

On 5 June 1967, Israel launched a preemptive air attack, Operation Focus, destroying 90% of the Egyptian air force on the ground. Secure in the air, the IDF fought a three-front war. In six days, Israel captured the Sinai Peninsula and Gaza Strip from Egypt, the West Bank and East Jerusalem from Jordan, and the Golan Heights from Syria. This massive victory established Israeli military dominance and created 300,000 new refugees.

Historical Interpretations: Contrasting Views

Interpretation 1: Michael Oren (Traditionalist / Self-Defense Focus)

"Israel was forced into a war of survival by a tightening ring of Arab aggression. Nasser's expulsion of UN peacekeepers, his massing of 100,000 troops on the border, and the illegal blockade of the Straits of Tiran were clear acts of war. Israel's pre-emptive strike on 5 June 1967 was a legitimate, necessary response to prevent its imminent annihilation."

Interpretation 2: Avi Shlaim (Revisionist / Opportunity Focus)

"Israel's military command knew that Egypt was not planning an imminent attack and utilized Nasser's rhetorical blunders as a golden opportunity to launch a pre-meditated war. By initiating the hostilities, Israel sought to crush the Arab militaries, expand its territorial borders, and establish undisputed regional hegemony in the Middle East."

Synthesis Tip: To secure high marks on the 1967 war, contrast the view that Israel faced a sudden existential crisis with the argument that Israel's military leaders saw Nasser's actions as a strategic opening.

1. What were the consequences of Israel's new occupied territories?

Israel's capture of the West Bank, Gaza, Sinai, and Golan Heights put 1 million Palestinians under military rule. Over 300,000 new refugees fled, mostly to Jordan. In November 1967, the UN passed Resolution 242, proposing 'Land for Peace'—Israeli withdrawal in exchange for security and recognition. Arab states rejected this, issuing the Three Nos at the Khartoum Conference: No peace, No recognition, No negotiations. Low-level artillery duels across the Suez Canal erupted into the War of Attrition (1967–70). Meanwhile, Israel began building early Jewish settlements in the occupied lands.

2. Why did the PLO turn to international terrorism?

Disillusioned with Arab state armies, Palestinians turned to militant self-reliance. In 1969, Yasser Arafat's Fatah group took control of the PLO. Radical factions like the PFLP (Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine) resorted to international terrorism to publicize their cause. In September 1970, the PFLP hijacked four international airliners and forced three to land at Dawson's Field in Jordan. After releasing passengers, they blew up the empty planes on television, causing global shock.

3. How did the PLO's expulsion from Jordan affect the conflict?

The Dawson's Field incident threatened Jordan's sovereignty. King Hussein ordered his army to crush the PLO's state-within-a-state, sparking a brutal civil war in September 1970 known as Black September. Thousands of Palestinians were killed, and the PLO was expelled. The PLO relocated its headquarters to Lebanon, establishing base camps in South Lebanon ('Fatahland') and Beirut to continue operations.

4. What was the impact of the Munich Olympics massacre?

Seeking revenge, a PLO splinter group calling itself Black September kidnapped and murdered 11 Israeli athletes at the Munich Olympics in September 1972. The massacre horrified the world. Israel responded with air raids on refugee camps and launched Operation Wrath of God, a covert Mossad campaign that assassinated PLO operatives across Europe, escalating international security concerns.

Historical Interpretations: Contrasting Views

Interpretation 1: Yezid Sayigh (State Sovereignty / Legalist Focus)

"King Hussein's crackdown on the PLO in Black September was a necessary defense of Jordanian sovereignty. No sovereign state can tolerate an armed, foreign militia operating as a 'state-within-a-state', launching unauthorized raids into a neighbor, and hijacking international airliners onto its soil. King Hussein acted to prevent the total collapse of his kingdom."

Interpretation 2: Rashid Khalidi (Palestinian Nationalist Focus)

"Black September was a brutal, disproportionate assault by the Jordanian monarchy to suppress the legitimate Palestinian national resistance. By crushing the PLO and killing thousands of civilians in camps, Jordan chose its own dynastic survival over Arab solidarity, pushing the armed struggle into Lebanon and sowing the seeds of the civil war in Beirut."

Synthesis Tip: Compare how state-focused historians view Black September as a law-and-order necessity, whereas Palestinian historians analyze it as a tragic blow to their national liberation movement.

1. Why did Sadat seek a new war with Israel?

Anwar Sadat succeeded Nasser in 1970, inheriting a bankrupt economy and occupied Sinai. To break the diplomatic deadlock and force the US to mediate, Sadat planned a limited war. To court the US, he expelled 15,000 Soviet advisors in 1972. He built a military alliance with Hafez al-Assad of Syria and secured Saudi commitments to use the oil weapon.

2. How did Egypt and Syria achieve initial success?

Israel was complacent, trusting the Bar-Lev Line—a massive 25-meter sand fortification along the Suez Canal. On 6 October 1973, during the holy day of Yom Kippur, Egypt and Syria launched a coordinated surprise attack. Egyptian forces breached the sand wall using high-pressure water cannons, crossing the canal, while Syrian tanks swept into the Golan Heights, catching the IDF off guard.

3. How did Israel recover from the surprise attack?

After heavy initial losses, Israel mobilized. The war became a superpower proxy struggle: the US launched a massive military airlift (Operation Nickel Grass) to resupply Israel, while the USSR supplied Arab forces. With fresh armor, the IDF pushed Syria back and crossed the Suez Canal, encircling Egypt's Third Army.

4. What was the global impact of the Arab oil embargo?

To force a ceasefire, Arab oil states launched the OPEC Oil Embargo, cutting production and banning exports to the US and Netherlands. Global oil prices quadrupled, triggering economic chaos. The superpowers pressured both sides to accept UN Resolution 338. The war ended in a stalemate, but it restored Arab pride, shattered Israeli complacency, and forced the US to launch shuttle diplomacy.

Historical Interpretations: Contrasting Views

Interpretation 1: Abraham Rabinovich (Military / Strategic Focus)

"The 1973 war ended as an outstanding military victory for Israel. Despite being caught completely by surprise on their holiest day, the IDF demonstrated incredible tactical flexibility. Within days, they repelled the Syrian tanks, crossed the Suez Canal, and surrounded the Egyptian Third Army, proving that Arab states could not defeat Israel militarily."

Interpretation 2: William B. Quandt (Diplomatic / Political Focus)

"The Yom Kippur War was a major strategic success for Egypt. President Sadat never aimed to conquer Israel; his goal was to shatter the myth of Israeli invincibility and break the diplomatic deadlock. By crossing the canal and forcing superpower intervention, Sadat restored Arab pride and forced the United States to actively broker peace."

Synthesis Tip: In GCSE exams, distinguish between tactical military outcomes and long-term diplomatic consequences. Israel won the tactical battles, but Egypt achieved its political goals.

1. How did the US try to broker peace after Yom Kippur?

In the aftermath of the 1973 war, US Secretary of State Henry Kissinger engaged in shuttle diplomacy, flying repeatedly between Middle East capitals. His efforts brokered disengagement agreements in 1974 and 1975, which separated Israeli and Egyptian forces in Sinai and Syrian forces in the Golan Heights. Crucially, these treaties returned the banks of the Suez Canal to Egyptian control, allowing it to reopen to global shipping in June 1975, reducing superpower tensions.

2. Why was Sadat's visit to Jerusalem so historic?

In 1977, Anwar Sadat took a massive gamble to break the diplomatic deadlock. On 19 November 1977, he became the first Arab leader to visit Israel, addressing the Knesset (Israeli parliament) in Jerusalem. He offered formal peace in exchange for the return of all occupied territories. In response, Israeli Prime Minister Menachem Begin visited Egypt in December 1977. These visits broke decades of psychological barriers and prepared the ground for formal negotiations.

3. What were the terms of the Camp David agreements?

To secure a treaty, US President Jimmy Carter invited Sadat and Begin to the presidential retreat at Camp David in September 1978. After 13 days of intense, secret negotiations, they signed the Camp David Accords. The accords established two frameworks: (1) a peace treaty returning Sinai to Egypt, and (2) a framework for Palestinian self-government (autonomy) in the West Bank and Gaza, which remained vague and unresolved.

4. Why did the peace treaty anger the Arab world?

On 26 March 1979, Sadat and Begin signed the formal Treaty of Washington, ending 30 years of war. Israel withdrew all troops and settlements from the Sinai Peninsula in exchange for Egyptian diplomatic recognition and security guarantees. However, the Arab world viewed Sadat as a traitor, expelling Egypt from the Arab League and cutting off financial aid. In October 1981, Sadat was assassinated by Islamist extremists in Cairo, though the peace treaty survived.

Historical Interpretations: Contrasting Views

Interpretation 1: Harold Saunders (Pro-Treaty / Diplomatic Success Focus)

"The Camp David Accords were a monumental breakthrough that transformed Middle Eastern diplomacy. By bringing Israel and Egypt together, President Carter successfully ended the threat of a multi-front war against Israel, dismantled the settlements in Sinai, and proved that negotiation could replace decades of bloodshed."

Interpretation 2: Edward Said (Critical / Palestinian-Centric Focus)

"Camp David was a catastrophic separate peace that abandoned the Palestinians. By removing Egypt—the strongest Arab military power—from the conflict, Israel was neutralized on its southern border. This left Israel free to expand settlements in the West Bank and launch its devastating invasion of Lebanon in 1982 with total impunity."

Synthesis Tip: When evaluating Camp David, consider both its success as a bilateral peace treaty and its failure to resolve the wider Arab-Israeli and Palestinian conflicts.

1. How did the PLO destabilize Lebanon?

After expulsion from Jordan, the PLO established a state-within-a-state in South Lebanon ('Fatahland'), launching rocket attacks into northern Israel. This armed presence destabilized Lebanon's fragile Christian-Muslim sectarian balance, helping drag Lebanon into civil war in 1975. In March 1978, after a PLO bus hijacking, Israel launched the Litani Operation, invading South Lebanon to push PLO bases back from the border.

2. Why did Israel invade Lebanon in 1982?

In June 1982, following the attempted assassination of the Israeli ambassador in London, Defense Minister Ariel Sharon launched Operation Peace for Galilee, a full-scale invasion of Lebanon. Israeli forces besieged West Beirut, eventually forcing Yasser Arafat and the PLO to withdraw to Tunis. However, in September 1982, Israel's allied Christian Phalangist militia entered the refugee camps of Sabra and Shatila and massacred hundreds of civilians. An Israeli inquiry (the Kahan Commission) found Ariel Sharon indirectly responsible, forcing his resignation. The invasion also led to the rise of a new Shiite militant enemy: Hezbollah.

3. What caused the outbreak of the First Intifada?

In December 1987, decades of military occupation erupted into the First Palestinian Intifada ('Uprising') in Gaza and the West Bank. This was a grassroots movement featuring civil disobedience, strikes, boycotts, and youth throwing stones at IDF tanks. The uprising was not controlled by the exiled PLO leadership but by local committees. A new Islamist militant organization, Hamas, was founded in Gaza during the uprising, challenging the secular PLO.

4. How did Israel's response damage its international standing?

Israeli Defense Minister Yitzhak Rabin ordered the IDF to suppress the uprising with physical force, implemented as the 'Iron Fist' policy (using force to break protesters' bones). The television coverage of heavily armed soldiers beating stone-throwing children drew international condemnation, turning global public opinion in favor of the Palestinian cause and forcing Israel to consider a political solution.

Historical Interpretations: Contrasting Views

Interpretation 1: Ahron Bregman (Israeli Defense / Security Focus)

"Israel's 1982 invasion of Lebanon was a necessary defensive action to eliminate the PLO's armed stronghold. For years, the PLO used 'Fatahland' in southern Lebanon to launch rocket strikes and terrorist raids on northern Israeli Galilee towns. The attempted assassination of Ambassador Argov was the final straw that forced Israel to secure its border."

Interpretation 2: Avi Shlaim (Critical / Pre-meditated Aggression Focus)

"Operation Peace for Galilee was an aggressive war of choice engineered by Defense Minister Ariel Sharon. The PLO had actually observed a ceasefire for a year. Sharon used the Argov shooting as a pretext to launch a pre-planned invasion, aiming to crush the PLO politically, destroy Palestinian national aspirations, and install a puppet Christian regime in Beirut."

Synthesis Tip: For questions on the Lebanon invasion, analyze whether the conflict was an immediate reaction to border provocations or part of a broader, pre-meditated geopolitical plan.

1. How did global changes push the PLO towards peace?

The end of the Cold War and the collapse of the Soviet Union cut off financial and military aid to Arab states. During the Gulf War (1991), Yasser Arafat isolated the PLO by backing Saddam Hussein's invasion of Kuwait, which led oil-rich Gulf states to cut off funding. As the sole superpower, the US used its leverage to bring both Israel and Arab states to the Madrid Conference in October 1991, establishing the principle of direct peace talks.

2. What breakthroughs were made at Oslo?

In 1992, the election of Yitzhak Rabin as Israeli PM opened new opportunities. In early 1993, secret negotiations took place in Norway. This led to Oslo I (the Declaration of Principles). Yasser Arafat renounced terrorism and recognized Israel's right to exist, while Rabin recognized the PLO as the representative of the Palestinians. On 13 September 1993, Rabin and Arafat shook hands on the White House lawn, hosted by Bill Clinton.

3. How were the Oslo Accords put into practice?

Oslo I established the Palestinian National Authority (PNA), granting self-rule in Gaza and Jericho. In October 1994, Rabin and King Hussein signed the Israel-Jordan Peace Treaty, ending a 46-year state of war. In September 1995, Oslo II was signed, dividing the West Bank into three zones: Area A (full Palestinian control), Area B (Palestinian civil, Israeli security control), and Area C (full Israeli control, containing Jewish settlements).

4. Why did the peace process ultimately stall?

The accords faced fierce opposition. Hamas launched suicide bombings to derail the peace process, while right-wing Israelis accused Rabin of treason. On 4 November 1995, at a peace rally in Tel Aviv, Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin was assassinated by Yigal Amir, a right-wing Jewish extremist. This tragedy froze the peace process, leaving permanent borders and statehood unresolved.

Historical Interpretations: Contrasting Views

Interpretation 1: Dennis Ross (Liberal / Optimistic Process Focus)

"The Oslo Accords were a vital, courageous breakthrough that ended decades of rejectionism. For the first time, Rabin and Arafat shook hands and recognized each other's legitimacy. It created the Palestinian Authority, initiated self-government, and established a realistic step-by-step framework that built trust toward a two-state solution."

Interpretation 2: Edward Said (Radical / Skeptical Post-Colonial Focus)

"The Oslo Accord was an instrument of Palestinian capitulation, a 'Palestinian Versailles.' It was a deeply flawed agreement that postponed all critical issues—Jerusalem, refugees, and borders—while creating a fragmented authority that acted as Israel's security policeman, allowing Israel to double its settlement population in the occupied lands."

Synthesis Tip: The Oslo Accords are highly debated. Some historians view them as a tragic, missed opportunity cut short by Rabin's assassination, while others see them as structurally designed to fail from the start.
