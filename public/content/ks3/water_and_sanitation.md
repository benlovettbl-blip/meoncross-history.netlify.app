## 1.1 Prehistoric and Roman Sanitation

Long before modern indoor plumbing existed, the inhabitants of prehistoric Britain had to find clever ways to secure drinking water and dispose of human waste. During the British Iron Age (spanning from 700 BC to the Roman invasion in AD 43), most people lived in small, roundhouse farming settlements such as Silchester. Without written documents from this period, archaeologists must carefully excavate the earth to piece together how these early societies survived. Their discoveries show that Iron Age families built their homes near fresh springs, streams, or hand-dug wells to secure their daily water. For waste disposal, they relied on basic earthen holes called cesspits. When these pits filled up with waste, people simply covered them over with a thick layer of soil and dug a fresh trench nearby.

The arrival of the Roman Empire in AD 43 completely transformed the British landscape and introduced revolutionary sanitation technology. The Romans believed that access to fresh, flowing water was essential for maintaining a healthy and orderly society. To supply their newly established towns and military fortresses, such as Corbridge, Roman engineers constructed massive stone channels known as conduits. These conduits used gravity to carry clean water over vast distances from natural springs directly into urban centres.

In Roman towns, this water fed grand public bathhouses, like the famous complex at Bearsden. These bathhouses were bustling social hubs where citizens exercised, relaxed, and washed in sequence through unheated, warm, and steaming hot chambers. Clean water also constantly flushed communal public toilets, known as latrines. At military sites like Housesteads Fort on Hadrian's Wall, soldiers sat together on stone benches above deep, stone-lined channels. A continuous stream of water beneath the seats swept waste away into underground sewers, keeping the fort clean and preventing the spread of disease. When the Romans finally withdrew around AD 410, much of this advanced engineering was abandoned, and Britain's sanitation slipped back into primitive patterns for centuries.

> "I am surrounded by all kinds of noise... picture to yourself the assortment of sounds, which are strong enough to make me hate my very powers of hearing! When the gentlemen are exercising with their lead weights... I hear their groans... and next, hear the screech of a hair-plucker... and the various cries of the sausage-seller, the baker, and the sweet-seller, who hawk their goods about the baths."
> — **Seneca the Younger, Roman Philosopher, *Epistulae Morales* (Letters to Lucilius), Letter 56, c. AD 62**

### Vocabulary
- **Cesspit**: A simple hole dug in the ground used by prehistoric and later societies to collect household sewage and human waste.
- **Conduit**: A stone channel or pipe designed by Roman engineers to transport clean water over long distances using gravity.
- **Latrine**: A communal Roman public toilet block, often flushed by a continuous stream of water running into underground sewers.

### True or False
- Iron Age families built their homes near fresh springs or hand-dug wells. (True)
- Roman bathhouses were private facilities used only by the Emperor. (False)
- Housesteads Fort contained communal toilets flushed by a continuous stream of water. (True)

### Timeline
- **AD 43**: Arrival of the Roman Empire in Britain and introduction of revolutionary sanitation technology.
- **AD 62**: Seneca the Younger writes about the loud noises surrounding Roman bathhouses.
- **AD 410**: Withdrawal of the Romans from Britain, leading to the abandonment of advanced sanitation systems.
