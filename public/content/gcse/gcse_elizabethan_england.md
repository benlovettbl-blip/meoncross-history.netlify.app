## The situation on Elizabeth's accession: KT 1.1

### Society and Government
**Elizabethan England in 1558: society and government** operated on a strict hierarchy known as the 'Great Chain of Being'. The government was centralised around the monarch and her 19-man Privy Council (led by William Cecil), while Parliament was called only occasionally, usually to pass laws or grant taxes.

*Significance:* The strict hierarchy ensured stability, but centralization meant the Queen relied heavily on a small group of trusted advisers, and conflict with Parliament could arise over taxes.

### The Virgin Queen
She immediately faced **The Virgin Queen: the problem of her legitimacy, gender, marriage**. Strict Catholics believed she was illegitimate because the Pope never officially recognised Henry VIII's marriage to Anne Boleyn. Furthermore, patriarchal 16th-century society believed women were too weak to rule or lead an army, creating immense pressure for her to marry and produce an heir.

*Significance:* Her gender and unmarried status created persistent political vulnerability, inviting both foreign suitors and Catholic plots centered on alternative claimants.

### Character and Strengths
Despite these doubts, **Her character and strengths** were formidable. She possessed a brilliant Humanist education, spoke multiple languages (including Latin and Greek), and masterfully used court patronage to command the absolute loyalty of her nobles.

*Significance:* Her intelligence and patronage system allowed her to control ambitious nobles and present an image of power and stability to the public.

### Challenges at Home and Abroad
She inherited a realm facing massive **Challenges at home and from abroad: financial weaknesses, religious divisions, the French threat**. The Crown was crippled by a £300,000 debt left by Mary I. England was torn apart by the Protestant/Catholic divide, and faced a direct geographical military threat from the 'Auld Alliance', which saw Catholic French troops stationed on the Scottish border.

*Significance:* The massive debt limited her ability to go to war, while the combination of religious divisions and French soldiers in Scotland made her early reign highly insecure.

## The 'settlement' of religion: KT 1.2

### The Religious Settlement
To solve the deep religious crisis, **Elizabeth's religious settlement (1559): its features and impact** aimed for a 'Middle Way' compromise. The Act of Supremacy appeased Catholics by naming her 'Supreme Governor' rather than 'Head', while the Act of Uniformity introduced an English prayer book but required priests to keep traditional Catholic-style vestments.

*Significance:* This compromise established the Church of England, allowing Protestant theology while preserving some Catholic ceremonies to prevent open rebellion.

### Clergy Impact
The immediate impact was highly successful with the ordinary clergy, as roughly 8,000 out of 10,000 priests took the oath of supremacy. However, Elizabeth was forced to dismiss and replace almost all Marian (Catholic) bishops with 27 new Protestant bishops to push the settlement through the House of Lords.

*Significance:* While the parish level accepted the change, the resistance of the bishops showed that Catholic devotion remained strong at the highest levels of the Church.

### Role of the Church
**The Church of England: its role in society** was a powerful tool for social and political control. Priests required a government preaching licence to spread royal messages, and Church Courts controlled the daily lives of the population by judging moral issues such as slander, wills, and marriage disputes.

*Significance:* The Church served as a civil service, ensuring obedience, spreading propaganda, and policing public morality on behalf of the Crown.

## Challenge to the religious settlement: KT 1.3

### The Puritan Challenge
**The nature and extent of the Puritan challenge** emerged rapidly from radical Protestants (including some of her own bishops) who demanded the removal of all surviving Catholic features. This triggered the Crucifix Controversy and the 1566 Vestment Controversy, which resulted in 37 Puritan priests losing their jobs for refusing to wear surplices.

*Significance:* It revealed deep divisions within the English Protestant camp, showing that zealots would refuse royal authority to purify the Church.

### The Domestic Catholic Challenge
Simultaneously, **The nature and extent of the Catholic challenge, including the role of the nobility, Papacy and foreign powers** intensified. In 1566, the Pope instructed English Catholics to boycott Protestant services, creating a massive 'recusant' movement (who paid a 1-shilling fine for non-attendance).

*Significance:* Catholic defiance shifted from passive resistance to active lawbreaking, establishing a loyalist opposition group (recusants) inside the country.

### The Foreign Catholic Threat
The foreign Catholic threat escalated when Philip II of Spain sent the brutal Duke of Alba and 10,000 troops to crush the Protestant Dutch Revolt in 1567, placing a massive, hostile Catholic army directly across the English Channel. Elizabeth responded by seizing the 'Genoese Loan' in 1568.

*Significance:* Spain's military presence near England increased Elizabeth's fear of invasion, leading her to financially provoke Spain by taking their gold.

## The problem of Mary, Queen of Scots: KT 1.4

### Mary's Claim to the Throne
**Mary, Queen of Scots: her claim to the English throne** was a profound threat to Elizabeth. As Henry VIII’s great-niece (granddaughter of Margaret Tudor), she had an unquestionable, legitimate Catholic bloodline, making her the rightful Queen in the eyes of many English Catholics.

*Significance:* Mary was a Catholic successor option, rendering any Catholic plot to assassinate Elizabeth immediately viable and dangerous.

### Arrival in England
Following the murder of her husband (Lord Darnley) and her forced abdication, Mary arrived in England in 1568. **Relations between Elizabeth and Mary, 1568–69** were highly tense.

*Significance:* Her physical presence in England created an active political crisis, forcing Elizabeth to decide whether to support a fellow monarch or imprison a rival.

### The Casket Letters and Captivity
Elizabeth set up the York Conference to investigate Mary using the 'Casket Letters', but deliberately ensured no verdict was reached. Because Mary was an 'anointed monarch', Elizabeth refused to execute her, instead keeping her in comfortable captivity where she immediately became a dangerous figurehead for Catholic plotters.

*Significance:* The lack of a verdict kept Mary's status ambiguous, containing the immediate political crisis but preserving a permanent catalyst for future rebellion.

## Plots and revolts at home: KT 2.1

### Revolt of the Northern Earls
**The reasons for, and significance of, the Revolt of the Northern Earls, 1569–70** stemmed from Catholic nobles (Northumberland and Westmorland) losing massive political power to Elizabeth's 'new men'. The rebels captured Durham Cathedral, celebrated Mass, and marched south with 4,600 men before fleeing. Its significance was marked by Elizabeth’s brutal execution of 800 rebels and the issuing of the 1570 Papal Bull, which excommunicated Elizabeth.

*Significance:* This was the first major domestic rebellion, leading to brutal suppression and direct papal interference that legitimised attempts to overthrow Elizabeth.

### Ridolfi, Throckmorton, and Babington Plots
**The features and significance of the Ridolfi, Throckmorton and Babington plots** escalated the foreign threat. Ridolfi (1571) planned to use 10,000 of Alba's troops, resulting in the execution of the Duke of Norfolk. Throckmorton (1583) revealed a French/Spanish invasion plan. Babington (1586) finally provided undeniable written evidence that Mary explicitly supported Elizabeth's assassination.

*Significance:* Each plot drew Spain closer to military action and convinced Parliament that Mary must be executed to secure Elizabeth's safety.

### Walsingham's Spy Network
**Walsingham and the use of spies** became vital. Walsingham used a vast European intelligence network, employing agents provocateurs, torture, and expert code-breakers (like Thomas Phelippes) to decipher the Babington ciphers.

*Significance:* Walsingham's sophisticated surveillance state successfully intercepted all attempts on Elizabeth's life, neutralizing threats before they could mobilize.

### Execution of Mary, Queen of Scots
**The reasons for, and significance of, Mary Queen of Scots’ execution in 1587** were driven directly by the Babington evidence. Her execution was significant because it permanently removed the domestic figurehead for English Catholics, but it gave Philip II of Spain the final religious justification he needed to launch the Armada.

*Significance:* The execution eliminated the internal threat but escalated the external threat by transforming a cold war with Spain into an active invasion conflict.

## Relations with Spain: KT 2.2

### Political, Religious and Commercial Rivalry
Anglo-Spanish relations broke down due to **Political, religious and commercial rivalry**. Philip II was a devout Catholic determined to stamp out Protestantism, signing the terrifying Treaty of Joinville with France in 1584. Commercially, Spain held a strict monopoly on the lucrative New World markets, which English merchants aggressively undermined.

*Significance:* The Treaty of Joinville united England's two main Catholic rivals (France and Spain), while English piracy in the Americas crippled Spanish finances.

### Drake and Privateering
**The significance of privateering and the activities of Drake** pushed the nations toward war. Secretly funded by Elizabeth, Drake plundered Spanish silver fleets. During his circumnavigation (1577-80), he claimed 'Nova Albion' (California) to defy the Pope, and returned with £400,000 of stolen Spanish treasure. Elizabeth’s decision to knight him on the deck of the *Golden Hind* was a massive public insult to Philip.

*Significance:* Drake's actions proved English naval potential, enriched the Crown, and directly provoked Spain by demonstrating Elizabeth's tacit endorsement of piracy.

## Outbreak of war with Spain, 1585-88: KT 2.3

### Involvement in the Netherlands
**Reasons for deteriorating relations with Spain: English direct involvement in the Netherlands and the actions of Robert Dudley** marked the beginning of an active war. ### Robert Dudley's Leadership
Dudley infuriated Elizabeth by accepting the title 'Governor-General' (implying England was taking over Spanish territory). Though the campaign failed due to underfunding, Dudley successfully defended the deep-water port of Ostend, preventing the Armada from safely docking later.

*Significance:* Dudley's actions caused diplomatic tension with Elizabeth, but the military defense of Ostend denied the Spanish Armada a critical deep-water port, impacting their strategy.

### The Raid on Cadiz
To buy time, **Drake and the raid on Cadiz: ‘Singeing the King of Spain’s beard’** took place in April 1587. Drake led a surprise attack on the Spanish fleet, destroying roughly 30 ships and thousands of vital barrel staves. This brilliant logistical sabotage successfully delayed the Armada by a full year.

*Significance:* The raid destroyed the seasoned barrel staves needed for food and water storage, ensuring Spanish provisions would rot and crippling their invasion timeline.

## The Armada: KT 2.4

### Spanish Invasion Plans
**Spanish invasion plans** were vast: Philip II sent an Armada of 130 ships and 30,000 men under the inexperienced Duke of Medina Sidonia to sail through the English Channel, pick up the Duke of Parma's 27,000 troops from the Netherlands, and march on London.

*Significance:* The plan relied on perfect communication and coordination between sea and land forces, a vulnerability that the English exploited.

### Key Events: Fireships at Calais
The **Key events of the Spanish Armada** saw the Spanish sail in a tightly-packed defensive crescent formation. The English broke this formation by sending eight burning fireships into the anchored fleet at Calais on the night of 6 August, forcing the Spanish to scatter in panic.

*Significance:* The fireships broke the Spanish defensive crescent, causing them to cut anchors and scatter, leaving them vulnerable to direct naval attack.

### Battle of Gravelines and Victory
**The reasons for the English victory** were confirmed at the Battle of Gravelines (8 August). The English won due to superior 'race-built' galleons equipped with quick-reloading culverin cannons, keeping their distance to batter the Spanish. Finally, the gale-force 'Protestant Wind' wrecked dozens of retreating Spanish ships off the Scottish and Irish coasts, leaving only 65 to return home.

*Significance:* The combination of superior naval technology, tactical intelligence, and extreme weather destroyed Spain's invasion plans, cementing England's Protestant independence.

## Education and leisure: KT 3.1

### Expansion of Education
**Education in the home and schools** expanded rapidly due to Humanist philosophies and the growth of trade. 72 new grammar schools were founded for wealthy boys (pushing male literacy to 30%), while girls were taught practical skills at home or in basic 'Dame Schools'. The 'Petty schools' taught basic literacy, and Oxford and Cambridge expanded.

*Significance:* Education remained highly class- and gender-divided, but the growth of schools reflected a rising gentry class and the need for literate merchants.

### Sports, Leisure, and Theatre
**Sport, pastimes and the theatre** thrived but were heavily divided by class. The nobility enjoyed exclusive sports like hawking, fencing, and real tennis, while the lower classes played violent, ruleless mob football. Purpose-built secular theatres (like The Theatre and The Rose) became a booming industry, protected from Puritan opposition by the patronage of nobles like the Earl of Leicester.

*Significance:* The expansion of theatre provided cheap entertainment and a social melting pot, serving as propaganda for the Queen while causing religious concern among Puritans.

## The 'problem' of the poor: KT 3.2

### Causes of Poverty
**The reasons for the increase in poverty and vagabondage during these years** were severe. The population boomed from 3 million to 4.2 million, causing massive inflation. Landowners adopted 'enclosure' and switched to lucrative sheep farming (accounting for 81.6% of exports), which required far less labour, causing mass rural unemployment. Bad harvests in 1573 and 1586 worsened starvation.

*Significance:* Population pressures combined with structural economic changes (wool trade growth) created a large, visible class of unemployed, wandering poor.

### Changing Poor Policies
**The changing attitudes and policies towards the poor** shifted from pure punishment to active local assistance. The 1572 Vagabonds Act ordered brutal punishments (whipping and boring a hole through the ear) but introduced a national poor rate. By the 1576 Poor Relief Act, local JPs were required to provide raw materials (like wool) for the able-bodied to work, and established 'Houses of Correction' for those who refused.

*Significance:* It showed the government began to distinguish between the 'deserving poor' (needing help) and 'idle poor' (needing punishment) to preserve social order.

## Exploration and voyages of discovery: KT 3.3

### Factors Prompting Exploration
**Factors prompting exploration, including the impact of new technology on ships and sailing and the drive to expand trade** drove sailors across the Atlantic. The loss of traditional cloth markets in Antwerp forced merchants to seek new luxury goods. *Significance:* Technological advances minimised sailing hazards, enabling voyages to discover alternative commercial routes and bypass Spanish trade blocks.

### Drake's Circumnavigation
**The reasons for, and significance of, Drake’s circumnavigation of the globe** (1577–80) originally focused on plundering Spanish colonies and seeking revenge for the attack at San Juan de Ulua. It was highly significant because it proved English maritime dominance, led to the political claim of 'Nova Albion', and returned a 4700% profit to its private investors, sparking an era of global ambition.

*Significance:* The voyage humiliated Spain, enriched the English Crown, and demonstrated that England had become a globally competitive naval power.

## Attempted colonisation of Virginia: KT 3.4

### Raleigh and the Colonisation Drive
**Reasons for the attempted colonisation of Virginia, including the significance of Raleigh**, focused on establishing a strategic North American base for privateers to attack Spanish treasure ships, and to secure valuable commodities (like tobacco and gold) independent of Europe. Sir Walter Raleigh secured the royal charter, raised the immense funds, and promoted the voyage, though the Queen forbade him from leading it.

*Significance:* Raleigh's planning established the template for future colonisation, attempting to establish an English military foothold in Spanish-dominated America.

### Failure of the First Settlement
**Reasons for the failure of the first settlement in Virginia** (1585) were a combination of terrible luck and poor planning. The vital supply ship, the *Tiger*, was breached in a storm, ruining the colonists' food and seeds. The settlers were the wrong mix of people (soldiers and gentlemen rather than farmers), which led to starvation, brutal demands on the local Algonquian tribes, and the outbreak of hostilities with Chief Wingina, forcing them to abandon the colony in 1586.

*Significance:* The failure demonstrated that military officers were ill-suited for colonization, requiring self-sustaining agricultural and cooperative local relations instead.
