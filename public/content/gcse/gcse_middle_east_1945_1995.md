## Topic 1.1: The British withdrawal and the creation of Israel

### Step 1: The Conflicting Demands
After the Second World War and the horrors of the Holocaust, the British Mandate became impossible to govern. Britain was caught in an impossible balancing act between two opposing forces:

          - **The Jewish/Zionist Demand:** Zionist leaders urgently demanded the creation of a Jewish state in Palestine to house European refugees. The USA strongly supported this, with President Harry Truman pressuring Britain to immediately allow 100,000 Jewish refugees into Palestine.

          - **The Palestinian Arab Demand:** Palestinian Arabs formed the majority of the population and violently opposed this, demanding immediate independence and an end to mass Jewish immigration. They were led by figures like the Grand Mufti of Jerusalem, **Haj Amin al-Husseini**.

          - **The British Dilemma:** Caught in the middle, British Foreign Secretary Ernest Bevin restricted Jewish immigration to a strict quota of just **1,500** people a month, fearing an Arab revolt that might threaten Britain's access to vital Middle Eastern oil.

        **So What?** This impossible balancing act alienated both sides, ultimately causing Jewish paramilitary groups to abandon their wartime truce and launch a violent insurgency to force the exhausted British to withdraw.

### Step 2: The Jewish Insurgency & The King David Hotel
Furious at the immigration restrictions, extremist Jewish groups like the **Irgun** and **Lehi** waged a violent terrorist campaign against British forces to force their withdrawal.

              - **The King David Hotel Bombing (22 July 1946):** The Irgun blew up the southern wing of the King David Hotel in Jerusalem, which housed the British military and administrative headquarters, killing **91** people. This severely damaged British morale and caused massive public outrage.

              - **The UN Referral (February 1947):** Exhausted by the violence and the immense financial cost of maintaining 100,000 troops, Britain referred the Mandate to the newly formed United Nations.

              - **The SS Exodus Crisis (July 1947):** British authorities turned away a refugee ship carrying 4,500 Holocaust survivors, returning them to Germany. This sparked massive international condemnation, particularly from the USA.

              - **The Sergeants Affair (July 1947):** In retaliation for British executions of Irgun members, the Irgun kidnapped, hanged, and booby-trapped the bodies of two British sergeants (Clifford Martin and Mervyn Paice). This triggered anti-Semitic riots in the UK and destroyed the domestic public's willingness to keep British troops in Palestine.

### Step 3: UN Resolution 181 (The Partition Plan)
On **29 November 1947**, the UN passed **Resolution 181**, which recommended dividing Palestine into two separate states.

              - **The Terms:** The Partition Plan proposed dividing Palestine into separate Jewish and Arab states, with **Jerusalem** kept under international control.

              - **The Jewish Reaction:** Jewish leaders reluctantly accepted the plan because it gave them international recognition for a sovereign state, even though they were disappointed Jerusalem was excluded.

              - **The Arab Reaction:** The Arab leadership completely rejected the plan. They felt the UN had no right to divide their land without the consent of the indigenous majority, especially since the proposed Jewish state was awarded over half (**55%**) of the territory even though Jews only made up one third (**33%**) of the population.

            **So What?** The UN's attempt to peacefully partition the land failed, sparking an immediate civil war between Arabs and Jews across the territory.

### Step 4: The 1948–49 Arab-Israeli War
As the British Mandate officially expired in May 1948, **David Ben-Gurion** declared the independent State of Israel on 14 May. The regular armies of five Arab states (Egypt, Syria, Jordan, Lebanon, and Iraq) invaded immediately.

              - **Why did Israel survive and win?**

                  - **Arab Disunity:** The invading armies were uncoordinated and lacked a single commander and unified goal.

                  - **Desperate Motivation & Tactics:** The Jewish forces were fighting desperately for survival with highly coordinated tactics.

                  - **Military Consolidation:** Prime Minister David Ben-Gurion merged various paramilitary forces (Haganah, Irgun, Lehi) into a single, unified national military: the **Israeli Defence Forces (IDF)**.

                  - **Truces & Czech Deal:** During ceasefires, Israel used the time to bring in modern weapons illegally from **Czechoslovakia** and double its troop numbers from **35,000** to **108,000**.

              - **UN Mediation & Bernadotte:** During the war, UN mediator **Count Folke Bernadotte** was sent to negotiate peace and truces, but he was assassinated in Jerusalem in September 1948 by the extremist Stern Gang.

            **So What?** By the time of the 1949 armistices, Israel won a decisive victory, expanding its borders to capture **50% more land** than originally allotted to the Arab state under the UN plan, and creating a massive Palestinian refugee crisis (the Nakba).

## Topic 1.2: Aftermath of the 1948-49 war

### Step 1: Territorial Changes and the New Map
As a result of the war, the proposed independent Arab state was completely wiped off the map.

              - **Israel's Expansion:** Israel captured **50% more land** than originally allocated (expanding its territory to cover **79%** of mandate Palestine), gaining control of vast new territories including the fertile **Galilee** region, the **Negev Desert**, and **West Jerusalem**.

              - **Jordan and Egypt:** Transjordan occupied the **West Bank** and East Jerusalem, annexing it in **1951**. Egypt took military control of the **Gaza Strip**.

              - **The Green Line:** The new borders were defined by the 1949 Armistice Agreements and became known as the **Green Line**.

### Step 2: The Palestinian Refugee Crisis (The Nakba)
The war was a disaster for Palestinian Arabs, who refer to the events of 1948 as the **Nakba** ('The Catastrophe').

              - **The Exodus:** Over **700,000** Palestinians fled or were forcibly expelled from their homes in the territory that became Israel.

              - **The Camps:** Most fled to squalid, overcrowded refugee camps in the Gaza Strip, West Bank, Lebanon, Syria, and Jordan. They lost their homes, land, and livelihoods, ending up dependent on international aid.

              - **UNRWA:** In December 1949, the UN established the United Nations Relief and Works Agency (**UNRWA**) to provide emergency food, health care, and schooling in these camps, ensuring their basic survival.

### Step 3: Consolidating the State of Israel
- **The Law of Return (1950):** In July 1950, Israel passed this law, granting any Jew in the world the right to immigrate and receive immediate citizenship. Israel's population doubled within three years, welcoming Holocaust survivors and Jews fleeing Arab countries. This massive demographic growth strained the fragile economy, leading to strict food rationing (**Tzena**) and tent cities.

              - **The IDF Conscription:** To secure the state during the fighting in May 1948, David Ben-Gurion forcibly merged pre-war militias (such as the **Haganah**, **Irgun**, and **Lehi**) into a single unified army: the **Israeli Defence Forces (IDF)**. In 1949, Israel introduced universal conscription, ensuring a massive reserve force to defend its borders.

              - **US Aid:** To prevent economic collapse, the Israeli government applied to the USA for urgent financial assistance, resulting in grants amounting to **$300 million** to help support and house the new Jewish immigrants.

            <strong style="display: block; margin-bottom: 6px; color: var(--primary);">📝 Source B: Israeli Government Statement (1950)</strong>
            <p style="font-style: italic; line-height: 1.4; color: var(--text-muted); margin: 0;">
              "This state will be open for Jewish immigration and for the Ingathering of the Exiles. It will promote the development of the country for the benefit of all its inhabitants."

### Step 4: Hostile Relations with Egypt
Following the war, relations between Israel and Egypt were characterized by deep hostility, resentment, and frequent violence, despite signing an armistice agreement in 1949.

              - **Resentment & Refugees:** Egypt bitterly resented Israel for its military defeat and for the massive burden of **200,000** Palestinian refugees now trapped in the Egyptian-controlled Gaza Strip.

              - **The Fedayeen Raids:** Palestinian militant 'freedom fighters' known as the **Fedayeen** launched frequent raids from Gaza into Israel to attack settlements, steal property, and kill civilians.

              - **IDF Reprisals:** The IDF responded with disproportionately harsh military reprisal raids against Egyptian and Palestinian targets in Gaza, steadily escalating the bloodshed and border tensions.

              - **Economic War:** Egypt launched an economic war against Israel, banning Israeli shipping from using the **Suez Canal** and blockading the **Straits of Tiran** (the Gulf of Aqaba), deliberately suffocating Israel's trade.

## Topic 1.3: Increased tension, 1955-63

### Step 1: Rise of Nasser and Pan-Arabism
- **Rise of Nasser (1954):** Colonel Gamal Abdel Nasser overthrew the corrupt King Farouk in a coup. He championed **Pan-Arabism**, a movement to politically unify Arab nations, protect Middle Eastern oil resources, and oppose Israel. He fiercely opposed Western imperialism, refusing to join the British and American-led **Baghdad Pact (1955)**, which he saw as an attempt to keep Arabs weak and divided.

          - **The Gaza Raid (28 February 1955):** The turning point in relations occurred when Israeli forces launched a fierce surprise attack on an Egyptian army base in **Gaza**, killing **38** Egyptian soldiers. This humiliating defeat exposed Egypt's military weakness.

          - **The Czech Arms Deal (September 1955):** Humiliated by the Gaza raid, Nasser sought to modernise his military. When Western powers refused to sell him weapons, he signed the **Czech Arms Deal**, importing modern Soviet fighter jets, tanks, and bombers, which shattered the balance of power in the region and alarmed Israel.

### Step 2: Israeli Border Raids and Operation Volcano (1955–1956)
In response to constant and deadly Fedayeen raids originating from Egyptian-controlled territory, Israel stepped up its military actions:

              - **Sinai Border Raids:** In late 1955 (including the major assault **Operation Volcano**) and into 1956, Israel launched aggressive raids into the Sinai Peninsula and demilitarized zones.

              - **The Objective:** These raids were designed to counter border infiltrations, destroy Egyptian military outposts, and weaken their defense readiness.

### Step 3: Nationalisation of the Suez Canal (July 1956)
Nasser wanted to build the massive **Aswan High Dam** on the River Nile to modernise Egypt's economy. In July 1956, after the USA and Britain abruptly withdrew their financial loans to punish Egypt for its ties to the Soviet bloc, Nasser retaliated immediately:

              - **The Nationalisation:** Nasser seized and nationalised the British and French-owned **Suez Canal Company**, declaring that Egypt would use the canal's transit tolls to fund the Aswan Dam. He also blockaded the Straits of Tiran to Israeli shipping.

              - **Western Fury:** Britain and France were outraged. They viewed the canal as a vital imperial lifeline for their oil shipping and saw Nasser as a dangerous dictator who had to be deposed.

### Step 4: The Protocol of Sèvres Collusion
- **The Secret Plan:** In October 1956, Britain, France, and Israel met secretly in France and signed the **Protocol of Sèvres**, a conspiracy to capture the canal and overthrow Nasser.

              - **The Pretext:** Under the plan, Israel would launch a surprise invasion of the Sinai Peninsula. Britain and France would then pretend to act as 'peacekeepers', ordering both sides to withdraw from the canal, and immediately invade to take control of it when Nasser refused.

### Step 5: Invasion and Superpower Intervention
On 29 October 1956, Israel launched the attack (Operation Kadesh), invading the Sinai Peninsula. The plan succeeded militarily, with Anglo-French paratroopers deploying to seize the canal zone, but resulted in a total political disaster for the West:

              - **US/USSR Opposition:** U.S. President Eisenhower was furious that his allies had launched a war without consulting him. The USA and the USSR applied massive economic and political pressure (including threats to crash the British pound) to force a ceasefire.

              - **UN Ceasefire & UNEF:** The UN condemned the invasion and forced Britain, France, and Israel into a humiliating withdrawal, replacing them with a peacekeeping buffer force: the **United Nations Emergency Force (UNEF)** in the Sinai.

              - **The Consequences:**

                  - **British/French Humiliation:** The crisis marked the end of Britain and France's status as global superpowers.

                  - **Nasser's Triumph:** Nasser achieved a monumental political victory for standing up to the West.

                  - **Israel's Gain:** Israel temporarily secured its southern border, stopped Fedayeen raids, and reopened shipping through the Straits of Tiran.

### Step 6: The United Arab Republic (UAR) and Pan-Arab Peak (1958–1963)
- **The UAR Union (February 1958):** At the height of his popularity, Nasser united Egypt and Syria to form a single nation, the **United Arab Republic (UAR)** under his presidency. This union represented the practical peak of **Pan-Arabism**, aiming to unify Arab states and isolate rival pro-Western monarchies.

          - **Israel's Fear of Encirclement:** The UAR caused severe security anxiety in Israel, which now faced a single united political entity on both its southern (Egypt) and northern (Syria) borders.

          - **Collapse of the Union (1961):** The UAR dissolved in 1961 after a military coup in Syria, but the anti-Israel rhetoric and the regional drive for Pan-Arab leadership remained high, keeping borders highly militarized until 1963.

## Topic 2.1: The Six Day War, 1967

### Step 1: The Cairo Conference and Jordan Water Crisis (1964)
- **The Cairo Conference (January 1964):** Arab League leaders met in Cairo. This summit was highly significant because it approved a plan to divert the headwaters of the River Jordan (the **Headwater Diversion Plan**) to restrict Israel's water supply, and officially established the **Palestine Liberation Organisation (PLO)** to coordinate resistance.

          - **The Airstrikes:** Israel viewed water as an existential necessity and launched repeated air strikes to destroy the Arab diversion works, initiating a period of intense border conflicts.

### Step 2: Border Skirmishes and the Samu Raid (1966–1967)
- **Fatah Raids:** Syria actively supported Yasser Arafat's guerrilla group, **Fatah**, providing training, weapons, and funds to launch raids into Israel from bases in neighbouring Jordan and Lebanon between 1965 and 1967.

          - **The Samu Raid (13 November 1966):** In retaliation for Fatah attacks, the IDF launched a massive armored raid on the Jordanian village of **Samu**, clashing with the Jordanian army, blowing up dozens of houses, and killing **15** Jordanian soldiers. This raid humiliated King Hussein of Jordan and pushed him to seek a mutual defense pact.

          - **Dogfight of 7 April 1967:** Border disputes over an Israeli tractor farming in the demilitarized zone escalated into a major artillery duel and dogfight. Israeli jets shot down six Syrian **MiG-21** fighter jets and boldly flew low over Damascus, humiliating the Syrian government.

### Step 3: Superpower Actions and the Straits Blockade (May 1967)
- **Soviet Misinformation (13 May 1967):** The USSR falsely warned President Nasser that Israel was massing troops on the Syrian border for an imminent invasion.

          - **Nasser's Brinkmanship:** Seeking to prove his Arab leadership, Nasser mobilized **100,000** troops in Sinai on 15 May, ordered the removal of UN peacekeepers (UNEF) on 18 May, closed the **Straits of Tiran** to Israeli shipping on 22 May (cutting off Israel's southern port of Eilat), and signed a mutual defense pact with Jordan on 30 May, placing Jordanian forces under Egyptian command.

          - **USA Actions:** The USA tried to organize an international flotilla (the 'Regatta' plan) to reopen the Straits, but ultimately refused to take direct military action, leaving Israel to feel it had to act alone to ensure its survival.

### Step 4: Operation Focus (5 June 1967)
Israel had warned that blockading the Straits of Tiran was a direct act of war. On the morning of **5 June 1967**, Israel launched a devastating preemptive strike called **Operation Focus**:

              - **Total Surprise:** Nearly the entire Israeli Air Force flew low over the Mediterranean under radar coverage, striking at 7:45 AM when Egyptian pilots were on breakfast break.

              - **Destruction of Arab Air Forces:** Within three hours, Israel destroyed **338** Egyptian aircraft on the ground, securing complete air superiority before ground troops even crossed the borders.

            <strong style="display: block; margin-bottom: 6px; color: var(--primary);">📝 Source A: Israeli Cabinet Statement (5 June 1967)</strong>
            <p style="font-style: italic; line-height: 1.4; color: var(--text-muted); margin: 0;">
              "Faced with Egyptian troop concentrations on our borders, the blockade of the Straits of Tiran, and the withdrawal of the UN forces, Israel has acted in self-defense to neutralize the imminent threat of destruction."

### Step 5: The Ground Campaigns on Three Fronts (5–10 June 1967)
- **The Sinai & Gaza Front:** Israeli ground forces breached Egyptian defenses, reaching the Suez Canal by 8 June and trapping the Egyptian army.

              - **The West Bank & Jerusalem Front:** After Jordan opened fire, the IDF counter-attacked, capturing the entire **West Bank** and **East Jerusalem** (including the Western Wall) by 7 June, reunifying the city.

              - **The Golan Heights Front:** On 9 June, Israeli troops scaled the steep volcanic cliffs of the **Golan Heights**, defeating the Syrian defenders and securing the border before a UN ceasefire on 10 June.

## Topic 2.2: Aftermath of the 1967 war

### Step 1: The Captured Territories & Refugee Crisis
- **The Occupied Territories:** During the war, Israel captured vast territories: the **Sinai Peninsula** and **Gaza Strip** from Egypt, the **West Bank** and **East Jerusalem** from Jordan, and the **Golan Heights** from Syria.

              - **The Significance:** These territories were highly significant: the **Golan Heights** controlled vital water headwaters; the **Sinai** and **Gaza** acted as security buffer zones; the **West Bank** provided strategic depth; and **East Jerusalem** held the holiest sites in Judaism (the Western Wall).

              - **New Refugee Wave:** The conquest triggered a second massive Palestinian refugee crisis, with over **300,000** Palestinians fleeing the West Bank and Gaza, mostly into Jordan, placing huge social and economic strains on neighboring Arab countries.

### Step 2: UN Resolution 242 & The War of Attrition (1967–70)
- **UN Resolution 242 (November 1967):** The UN Security Council passed a resolution calling for Israel's withdrawal from territories occupied in the conflict in exchange for Arab states recognizing Israel's right to exist in peace (the 'Land for Peace' formula).

              - **The Khartoum Summit:** In August 1967, Arab leaders issued the 'Three Nos': no peace, no recognition, and no negotiations with Israel, causing a total diplomatic deadlock.

              - **The War of Attrition (1967–1970):** This deadlock led to a continued dispute over the Suez Canal. Egyptian President Nasser launched a low-intensity war of attrition, involving heavy artillery bombardment, commando raids, and aerial dogfights. The canal remained closed to global shipping, draining resources and leading to Soviet pilots flying combat missions for Egypt.

### Step 3: PFLP Hijackings & Expulsion from Jordan (1970)
- **A Shift to Terror:** Frustrated by the military defeat of Arab states, the **PFLP** (Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine) turned to international terrorism to force global attention onto the Palestinian issue.

          - **Dawson's Field Hijackings (September 1970):** The PFLP hijacked four Western passenger aircraft and flew them to Dawson's Field in Jordan, blowing up three empty jets on live television.

          - **International Attitudes:** The spectacular nature of these hijackings brought the Palestine issue onto the front pages of world news, raising global awareness of the refugee plight but also alienating Western public opinion due to the violence.

          - **Black September & PLO Expulsion:** Outraged by this challenge to his sovereignty, Jordan's King Hussein launched a brutal military crackdown in September 1970. The Jordanian army expelled the PLO, killing thousands of fighters and forcing the PLO to relocate its headquarters to Lebanon.

### Step 4: The Munich Olympics Massacre (1972)
- **The Attack:** A radical Palestinian splinter group named **Black September** broke into the Olympic Village during the 1972 Munich games and took the Israeli athletic team hostage.

              - **The Outcome:** A botched German rescue attempt led to the deaths of all 11 Israeli athletes.

              - **The Repercussions:** The massacre succeeded in gaining massive global publicity for the Palestinian cause, but it horrified the world and resulted in Israel launching fierce, targeted reprisal assassinations (Operation Wrath of God).

## Topic 2.3: Israel and Egypt, 1967-73

### Step 1: Relations and Consolidation (1967–73)
- **Sadat's Realignment:** Anwar Sadat succeeded Nasser in 1970. To court the **USA** and force a diplomatic solution, he expelled **15,000** Soviet military advisers in 1972. He secretly rebuilt relations with other **Arab states**, coordinating with Syria for a two-front war and securing Saudi commitment to use oil as a diplomatic weapon.

          - **Israel's Consolidation:** Israel consolidated its control of the occupied territories. It built the **Bar-Lev Line** (a massive 25-metre sand wall fortification along the Suez Canal) and established the first civilian Jewish settlements in the Sinai, West Bank, and Golan Heights to assert long-term control.

### Step 2: Coordinated Surprise & Canal Crossing (October 1973)
- **Coordinated Attack:** On **6 October 1973**, Egypt and Syria launched a surprise assault on **Yom Kippur** (the holiest day in Judaism), catching Israel unprepared.

              - **Breaching the Canal:** Egyptian troops crossed the Suez Canal, using high-pressure **water cannons** to blast holes in the sand wall.

              - **Missile Defense:** Soviet-supplied surface-to-air missiles (SAMs) shot down Israeli jets, shielding the Egyptian bridgehead from counter-attacks.

### Step 3: Superpower Confrontation & IDF Counter-Offensive
The local war escalated into a dangerous Cold War superpower crisis:

              - **The Airlifts:** When Israel suffered massive tank and aircraft losses, U.S. President Nixon launched a huge emergency airlift (**Operation Nickel Grass**). The **USSR** launched a matching supply lift to Egypt and Syria.

              - **Israeli Counter-Crossing:** Armed with U.S. supplies, the IDF counter-attacked. Israeli divisions led by Ariel Sharon crossed the Suez Canal into Egypt, surrounding the Egyptian Third Army. This prompted a tense superpower standoff, leading the US to put its nuclear forces on alert (DEFCON 3).

### Step 4: The OPEC Oil Crisis and Ceasefire (1973)
- **The Oil Embargo:** Furious at U.S. military support for Israel, Arab oil ministers cut production and embargoed oil exports to the West, quadrupling prices. This caused fuel shortages, electricity rationing, and recession in the USA and Europe.

              - **UN Resolution 338:** To avoid direct conflict, the USA and USSR jointly negotiated a ceasefire under **Resolution 338**.

              - **The Aftermath:** Egypt held its canal positions, restoring Arab pride, while Israel realized its security could not rely purely on military force. This paved the way for future peace negotiations.

## Topic 3.1: Diplomatic negotiations

### Step 1: The Global Oil Crisis & Superpower Rivalry
- **The Global Impact:** The OPEC oil embargo of 1973 quadrupled oil prices, causing severe inflation, energy shortages, and recession in Western economies. This crisis made stabilizing the Middle East a critical priority for the West.

          - **Superpower Involvement:** The Cold War rivals played opposing roles. The **Soviet Union** backed Arab regimes with weapons to expand its influence. In contrast, the **USA** took the diplomatic lead to protect Western oil supplies, exclude the Soviets, and bring Egypt into the American sphere of influence.

### Step 2: Kissinger's Shuttle Diplomacy (1974–75)
- **The Mediator:** U.S. Secretary of State **Henry Kissinger** conducted intensive travel between Middle Eastern capitals to broker agreements. Because Arab states refused to sit in the same room as Israelis, Kissinger literally flew back and forth (hence **Shuttle Diplomacy**).

              - **Early Success:** He successfully negotiated disengagement agreements, separating the armies. This led to Egypt reopening the **Suez Canal** to global shipping in June 1975.

              - **Sadat in Jerusalem (Nov 1977):** Sadat traveled to Jerusalem and addressed the **Knesset** (Israeli Parliament), offering peace in exchange for occupied lands. He became the first Arab leader to recognize Israel, breaking the Khartoum 'Three Nos'.

              - **Begin in Egypt (Dec 1977):** Prime Minister **Menachem Begin** reciprocated by visiting Sadat in Ismailia, Egypt. This return visit proved that bilateral negotiations had replaced direct military hostility.

## Topic 3.2: The Palestinian issue

### Step 1: Arafat at the UN & 'Fatahland'
- **The Olive Branch:** In 1974, Yasser Arafat addressed the UN, famously stating he had come bearing an olive branch and a freedom fighter's gun. This resulted in the PLO gaining international recognition and UN observer status.

          - **Lebanon:** After being expelled from Jordan in 1970, the PLO moved its headquarters to Lebanon. They established a 'state within a state' (nicknamed **Fatahland**) in southern Lebanon, where they launched rocket attacks and raids into northern Israel.

          - **Escalation:** The PLO's presence upset the balance of power in Lebanon, which contributed to the outbreak of the Lebanese Civil War in 1975, and provoked frequent Israeli air strike reprisals.

### Step 2: The Invasion of Lebanon (1982)
- **The Trigger:** In June 1982, following an assassination attempt on the Israeli ambassador in London by a Palestinian splinter group, Israel launched a massive invasion called **Operation Peace for Galilee**.

              - **Sharon's Plan:** The official aim was to push the PLO back 40km to create a buffer zone for northern Israel, but Defence Minister **Ariel Sharon** secretly planned to destroy the PLO completely and install a friendly Christian government in Beirut.

              - **The Siege of Beirut:** The IDF rapidly bypassed UN peacekeepers and surrounded the Lebanese capital. Israel subjected West Beirut to a devastating two-month bombardment, cutting off food and water. This forced the PLO to agree to evacuate; Arafat and approximately 14,000 fighters were exiled to **Tunis** under international supervision.

### Step 3: The Sabra and Shatila Massacres (1982)
- **The Atrocity:** In September 1982, following the assassination of the Lebanese President, Lebanese Christian Phalangist militias entered the **Sabra and Shatila** refugee camps and brutally massacred hundreds of Palestinian and Lebanese civilians over three days.

              - **Israel's Complicity:** Although the Phalangists carried out the killings, the IDF controlled the area and had allowed the militias into the camps.

              - **The Fallout:** Israel’s own **Kahan Commission** found the government indirectly responsible. This resulted in massive anti-war protests inside Israel, forced Ariel Sharon to resign as Defence Minister, and severely damaged Israel's international reputation.

### Step 4: The First Intifada (1987–93)
- **The Spark:** In December 1987, spontaneous mass protests broke out after an Israeli army truck killed four Palestinians in Gaza. Decades of frustration under Israeli occupation exploded into a grassroots uprising known as the **Intifada** (meaning 'shaking off').

              - **David vs. Goliath:** Palestinian youths famously fought heavily armed Israeli soldiers by throwing stones and petrol bombs. The uprising also featured mass civil disobedience, strikes, and boycotts of Israeli goods.

              - **The Iron Fist:** Under the government of Likud Prime Minister **Yitzhak Shamir**, Israel's Defence Minister Yitzhak Rabin responded with a harsh **'Iron Fist' policy** (using tear gas, beatings, and live ammunition) to suppress the uprising, drawing massive international condemnation.

              - **Rise of Hamas:** The uprising led to the emergence of a new, radical Islamic militant group called **Hamas**, which challenged the PLO's leadership and rejected compromise.

## Topic 3.3: Attempts at a solution, 1974-95

### Step 1: Arafat Renounces Terrorism (1988)
- **The Speech:** Yasser Arafat addressed the UN in **Geneva** and announced a massive shift in PLO policy. He formally recognised Israel's right to exist, accepted UN Resolution 242 (the 'Land for Peace' formula), and officially renounced the use of terrorism.

          - **The Impact:** This major concession resulted in the USA agreeing to open diplomatic dialogue with the PLO for the first time, breaking a long-standing political deadlock.

### Step 2: Changing Superpower Policies (1989–1991)
- **End of the Cold War:** Soviet reforms under **Mikhail Gorbachev** ended the Cold War, and the collapse of the **USSR** meant the PLO lost its main supplier of weapons and funding. Meanwhile, 200,000 Soviet Jews migrated to Israel, increasing pressure on Palestinian land. Global events severely weakened the PLO's bargaining position and forced them to negotiate.

          - **The Gulf War (1991):** Arafat disastrously supported Saddam Hussein's invasion of Kuwait. This caused angry Arab states (like Saudi Arabia and Kuwait) to cut off all financial aid to the PLO, leaving them bankrupt.

          - **The Madrid Conference:** U.S. President **George H.W. Bush** emerged from the 1991 Gulf War as the leader of the sole remaining superpower and used this leverage (including threatening to withhold $10 billion in loan guarantees) to force Yitzhak Shamir and Arab nations to negotiate at the **Madrid Peace Conference**.

### Step 3: The Oslo I Accords (1993)
- **Secret Talks:** Guided by key PLO negotiator **Mahmoud Abbas**, highly secret back-channel negotiations in Norway led Israeli Prime Minister **Yitzhak Rabin** and Yasser Arafat to sign the Oslo I Accords in Washington in September 1993.

              - **Mutual Recognition:** The PLO formally recognised the State of Israel, and Israel recognised the PLO as the legitimate representative of the Palestinian people.

              - **The PNA:** The agreement created the **Palestinian National Authority (PNA)**, giving Palestinians limited self-rule, initially in the Gaza Strip and the West Bank town of Jericho.

              - **Peace with Jordan:** The optimism of Oslo led to **Jordan** becoming the second Arab state to sign a full peace treaty with Israel in 1994.

### Step 4: Oslo II (1995) and the Collapse of Peace
- **Oslo II:** In 1995, the second Oslo agreement divided the West Bank into **Areas A, B, and C**, giving the PNA varying levels of control and preparing for democratic Palestinian elections.

              - **Hamas Opposition:** Radical Islamic militant groups like **Hamas** completely rejected the peace deal and launched devastating suicide bombings inside Israel to derail it.

              - **Israeli Opposition:** Right-wing Israelis and settlers were furious at the prospect of giving up 'Promised Land' to the Palestinians. They viewed Prime Minister Rabin as a traitor.

              - **Rabin's Assassination:** In November 1995, Yitzhak Rabin was assassinated at a peace rally by an Israeli religious extremist, **Yigal Amir**. This horrific event shattered the optimism of the Oslo Accords and brought the peace process to a grinding halt.
