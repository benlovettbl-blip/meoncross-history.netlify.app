📝 Master the Exam: Paper 2, Section B (Early Elizabethan England)

This section is worth 20% of your total GCSE. To achieve top marks, you need to master three specific question types. Use this mark-ladder to understand exactly what the examiner is looking for!

🏗️ Q1(a) & Q1(b): Describe one feature... (2 Marks each, 4 Marks total)

  • The Formula: Two separate 2-mark questions. For each question: Identify/state the feature (1 mark) + Add one specific detail/fact to support it (1 mark).
  • The Technique: Keep it surgical. Write a short, direct statement. Do not write a massive paragraph.
  • Example: "One feature of Elizabethan theatres was the pit (1 mark), where the poorer 'groundlings' stood to watch the play for just one penny (1 mark)."

⚖️ Q(b): Explain why... (12 Marks)

  • The Formula: 3 x PEEL Paragraphs (Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link).
  • The Technique: This is a causation question. Do not just tell a story! You are given two stimulus points, but you must include a third point of your Own Knowledge to reach the top marks.
  • The Glue: The "L" in PEEL is crucial. You must link back to the question. Instead of just saying "This happened," use phrases like "This exacerbated the tension because..." or "This directly led to the failure because...".

🏆 Q(c) (i) or (ii): 'How far do you agree?' (16 Marks)

  • The Formula: Introduction (Thesis) ➔ Paragraph 1 (Agree) ➔ Paragraph 2 (Disagree/Alternative) ➔ Conclusion (Sustained Judgement).
  • The Technique: This question requires a judgement. Do not sit on the fence! Plan your answer in two columns (For and Against) before you start writing to weigh up the evidence.
  • Own Knowledge: Just like the 12-mark question, you must use information of your own alongside the provided stimulus points to access the highest grades.

⏱️ Top Examiner Tips

  • Chronology is Key: A secure understanding of chronology is fundamentally important. Always carefully check the date ranges used in the questions so you don't write about the wrong events!
  • Manage Your Time: Don’t write too much for the 4-mark and 12-mark questions. Use your time wisely so you have enough time to plan and write your 16-mark essay.
  • Never Leave a Blank: Aim to write something for every question, even if it is just general knowledge about the topic: every single mark counts towards your final grade!
