Teacher Answer Key & Explanations

For Teacher Use Only

Lesson 1: The End of the British Mandate and the Creation of Israel, 1945–1949

  • Q1. Q1: What was the 'dual obligation' that Britain faced under the League of Nations Mandate of Palestine? (P1)
    Model Answer:
    Britain was bound by a 'dual obligation': to establish a 'national home' for the Jewish people, and on the other hand, to safeguard the civil and religious rights of the existing Arab majority.
  • Q2. Q2: Identify two reasons why Foreign Secretary Ernest Bevin set a strict immigration quota of 1,500 Jewish immigrants per month. (P4)
    Model Answer:
    First, Bevin calculated that allowing a massive influx of Jewish refugees would trigger an uncontrollable civil war. Second, Britain was economically dependent on maintaining close relations with oil-rich Arab states that opposed unrestricted Jewish immigration.
  • Q3. Q3: How did the United Nations Partition Plan (Resolution 181) divide the land of Palestine, and what was the reaction of the two communities? (P13)
    Model Answer:
    Resolution 181 allocated 55% of Palestine to the Jewish population and 45% to the Arab majority, with Jerusalem as an international zone. The Zionist leadership accepted the plan with joy, while Arab leaders vehemently rejected it and vowed to fight to prevent the partition of their homeland.
  • Q4. Q4: Describe the tactical advantage Israel gained during the first UN truce in June 1948. (P19)
    Model Answer:
    Israel used the one-month truce to unify its forces into a single national army (the IDF) and illegally import modern military hardware from Czechoslovakia, including 30,000 rifles and 84 combat aircraft. This completely neutralized the Arabs' initial material superiority.
  • Q5. Q5: Explain the long-term consequence of the 1948-49 war for the Palestinian Arab population. (P21)
    Model Answer:
    The war resulted in the Nakba ('The Catastrophe'), where over 700,000 Palestinian Arabs were permanently displaced from their homes, becoming refugees in neighboring states and losing a sovereign homeland.

Lesson 2: The Aftermath of the 1948–49 War

  • Q1. Explain one consequence of the territorial changes following the 1948–49 Arab-Israeli war. (4 marks)
    Model Answer:
    One consequence of the territorial changes following the 1948–49 Arab-Israeli War was the permanent displacement of the Palestinian Arab population and the creation of a massive refugee crisis. Prior to the war, the UN Partition Plan had allocated 45% of the land to a proposed Arab state; however, Israel's military victory expanded its borders to cover 79% of mandate Palestine, while Jordan occupied and annexed the West Bank and Egypt took control of the Gaza Strip. As a direct result of these territorial changes, over 700,000 Palestinian Arabs lost their homes and became stateless refugees. They were forced to resettle in squalid, overcrowded refugee camps managed by UNRWA in the Gaza Strip, West Bank, Jordan, and Lebanon, where they were denied the "right of return" by Israel, cementing a permanent humanitarian crisis that remains unresolved.

Lesson 3: Increased Tension, 1955–1963

  • Q1. Explain one consequence of the nationalisation of the Suez Canal in 1956. (4 marks)
    Model Answer:
    One consequence of the nationalisation of the Suez Canal in 1956 was the secret military collusion between Great Britain, France, and Israel, which led directly to the Suez Crisis. Following Nasser’s seizure of the canal to fund his Aswan High Dam project, Britain and France, who were the primary shareholders, feared their vital oil supply lines were threatened by a hostile Egyptian nationalist leader. As a result, they signed the secret Protocol of Sèvres with Israel, planning a tripartite military invasion where Israel would attack Sinai, providing a pretext for Anglo-French troops to land at Port Said to "protect" the canal. This consequence escalated border tensions into a major regional war on 29 October 1956, bringing the Cold War superpowers directly into the Middle Eastern conflict.

Lesson 4: The Six Day War, 1967

  • Q1. Write a narrative account analysing the key events of the May 1967 crisis that led to the outbreak of the Six Day War. (8 marks)
    Model Answer:

    The May 1967 crisis began with false intelligence provided by the Soviet Union on 13 May, which warned Egyptian President Nasser that Israel was massing ten military brigades on the Syrian border for an imminent invasion. Consequently, Nasser reacted by taking immediate military action, mobilizing the Egyptian army on 15 May and marching his troops into the demilitarised Sinai Peninsula.

    This aggressive move was quickly followed by Nasser’s order on 16 May forcing the United Nations Emergency Force (UNEF) peacekeepers to withdraw from the border. With tensions already at a fever pitch, Nasser escalated the crisis further on 23 May by reoccupying Sharm el-Sheikh and ordering the closure of the Straits of Tiran to all Israeli shipping, a move Israel considered a formal casus belli.

    This economic threat was compounded on 30 May when King Hussein of Jordan flew to Cairo to sign a joint defense pact. This final step convinced the Israeli Cabinet that a coordinated, three-front Arab invasion was imminent. This sequence of events culminated on the morning of 5 June 1967, when Israel launched its pre-emptive air strike, Operation Focus, destroying the Egyptian air force on the ground and initiating the Six Day War.

Lesson 5: The Aftermath of the 1967 War

  • Q1. Write a narrative account analysing the key developments in the Palestinian issue in the years 1970–72. (8 marks)
    Model Answer:

    The escalation of Palestinian militant activity began in September 1970, when the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP) launched a campaign of international aviation terrorism by hijacking commercial airliners and blowing them up at Dawson’s Field in Jordan. This event acted as the immediate trigger for the 'Black September' crisis, prompting King Hussein to launch an all-out offensive that successfully crushed the militias and expelled the PLO from Jordan by July 1971.

    As a direct consequence of this expulsion, the PLO relocated its headquarters to Lebanon, establishing a new military base in the south. Seeking revenge for their defeat in Jordan and desperate for global recognition, a radical PLO splinter group calling itself 'Black September' was formed. This development culminated in the Munich Olympics attack in September 1972, where terrorists took Israeli athletes hostage, resulting in the deaths of 11 Israeli team members during a failed West German rescue attempt.

    Ultimately, this tragic event drew widespread international condemnation of Palestinian tactics but forced the Palestinian issue onto the global diplomatic stage. In response to the Munich massacre, Israeli Prime Minister Golda Meir authorized a severe reprisal policy, launching 'Operation Wrath of God'—a targeted global assassination campaign to hunt down the conspirators—which locked both sides into a violent shadow war.

Lesson 6: Israel and Egypt, 1967–1973

  • Q1. Write a narrative account analysing the key developments of the Yom Kippur War (1973) and its aftermath. (8 marks)
    Model Answer:

    The Yom Kippur War began on 6 October 1973, when Egypt and Syria launched a coordinated surprise attack on the holiest day in the Jewish calendar. Because the country was at a complete standstill, Israeli military mobilization was severely delayed, allowing Egyptian troops to easily overrun the outer sand fortifications of the Bar Lev Line while Syrian forces seized the Golan Heights.

    This critical initial breakthrough prompted the United States to launch a massive military airlift on 12 October to resupply the heavily depleted Israeli forces. As a direct result of this American military support, the IDF successfully launched a decisive counter-offensive, driving Syrian forces back and enabling General Ariel Sharon’s tank divisions to cross the Suez Canal to encircle Egypt’s Third Army.

    With Egypt facing complete military collapse, the crisis escalated into a dangerous Cold War confrontation as the Soviet Union threatened to deploy troops, forcing the US to place its military on nuclear alert. To pressure the West into restraining Israel, Arab members of OPEC implemented the 'oil weapon' by placing a total oil embargo on nations supporting Israel. Ultimately, this economic pressure forced the United States to compel Israel to agree to a UN ceasefire on 25 October 1973. The long-term consequence of this conflict was a profound psychological shift: it shattered the myth of Israeli invincibility and paved the way for 'shuttle diplomacy'.

Lesson 7: Diplomatic negotiations, 1974–1979

  • Q1. Explain one consequence of the 1973 Oil Crisis for diplomatic negotiations in the Middle East. (4 marks)
    Model Answer:

    One consequence of the 1973 Oil Crisis was that it forced the United States to abandon its policy of indifference and actively intervene as a neutral mediator to negotiate peace in the Middle East. Following the Yom Kippur War, Arab members of OPEC implemented an oil embargo and quadrupled oil prices from $3 to $12 a barrel to punish the West for supporting Israel, plunging the US economy into severe inflation and high unemployment.

    As a direct consequence of this economic shock, US Secretary of State Henry Kissinger launched a tireless campaign of 'shuttle diplomacy'. Kissinger flew continuously between Middle Eastern capitals to broker military disengagement treaties (Sinai I and II), which successfully eased regional tensions and directly led to Egypt reopening the strategically vital Suez Canal in June 1975.

Lesson 8: The Palestinian Issue, 1974–1993

  • Q1. Write a narrative account analysing the key developments of the PLO in Lebanon in the years 1970–82. (8 marks)
    Model Answer:

    Following their crushing military defeat and subsequent expulsion from Jordan during the Black September civil war of 1970, the PLO relocated their central headquarters to Lebanon. This relocation was highly significant because Lebanon's weak central government and its large population of 400,000 Palestinian refugees allowed Yasser Arafat to establish a powerful "state-within-a-state" in southern Lebanon, which became widely known as "Fatahland". From these secure bases, Fatah launched frequent rocket attacks and sabotage raids into northern Israel, deeply threatening Israeli security and provoking a cycle of violent border reprisals.

    This escalating border conflict reached a critical turning point in March 1978, when a Fatah commando unit conducted the Coastal Road Massacre, killing 37 Israeli civilians. In direct response to this atrocity, Israel launched Operation Litani, occupying southern Lebanon up to the Litani River. Although Israel subsequently withdrew under UN pressure, the deployment of UNIFIL peacekeepers failed to halt the violence. The conflict was pushed to a final crisis on 3 June 1982, when the attempted assassination of Shlomo Argov, the Israeli ambassador in London, provided the immediate pretext for Defense Minister Ariel Sharon to launch a full-scale invasion.

    This invasion, codenamed Operation Peace for Galilee, quickly escalated as IDF armored divisions bypassed UN lines, defeated Syrian forces, and completely encircled the capital city of West Beirut. The resulting two-month Siege of West Beirut was highly important because the intense military and diplomatic pressure forced the PLO to surrender. This sequence of events culminated in late August 1982, when Yasser Arafat and over 14,000 PLO fighters were evacuated by a multinational force to Tunis, Tunisia, leaving the PLO geographically isolated 2,400 kilometers away from Israel's borders.

Lesson 9: Attempts at a solution, 1988–1995

  • Q1. Write a narrative account analysing the key developments in the peace process between Israel and the Palestinians in the years 1988–95. (8 marks)
    Model Answer:

    The peace process began in December 1988 when PLO Chairman Yasser Arafat, who sought to regain political initiative from the grassroots leaders of the First Intifada, delivered a historic speech to the United Nations General Assembly in Geneva. By explicitly renouncing terrorism and officially recognizing Israel’s right to exist, Arafat fulfilled the strict conditions required for diplomatic dialogue with the United States. Consequently, this diplomatic breakthrough, combined with the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991, severely weakened the PLO's bargaining power and forced both Israel and the Arab states to participate in the Madrid Peace Conference in November 1991.

    Although the formal public negotiations in Madrid and Washington ended in stalemate, the election of Yitzhak Rabin’s moderate Labor government in 1992 enabled the opening of a secret, back-channel negotiation in Norway. These secret meetings were accelerated in April 1993 when Hamas launched its first suicide car bombing in a West Bank settlement, shocking Israeli society and convincing Rabin that he needed to strengthen the secular PLO before radical Islamists captured Palestinian public opinion. As a result of this mutual urgency, the two sides signed the historic Oslo I Accords on the White House lawn on 13 September 1993, which established the Palestinian National Authority (PNA) to govern Gaza and Jericho.

    This breakthrough finally culminated in the signing of the Oslo II Accords on 28 September 1995, which expanded Palestinian self-rule across the West Bank by dividing the territory into Areas A, B, and C. However, this complex division triggered intense domestic opposition on both sides, leading to Hamas launching a campaign of suicide bus bombings and right-wing Israelis condemning Rabin's concessions. This sequence of events reached a tragic climax on 4 November 1995, when Rabin was assassinated by a right-wing Jewish extremist, abruptly halting the peace process and leaving the status of Jerusalem, refugees, and settlements completely unresolved.
