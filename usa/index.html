Edexcel Paper 3: Exam Technique Guide

Master the exact recipe for every question type in the GCSE USA (1954–75) Depth Study. The total exam length is 1 hour and 30 minutes (90 minutes). Select a question type below to reveal structure frames, timing guides, and model templates.

Question 1: Source Inference (4 Marks)
5 Mins

The Recipe

Give two separate inferences supported by details. You must state the inference clearly, and quote or describe details from the source to prove it.

Writing Frame

1. "I can infer from Source A that [Inference 1]. This is supported by the source details: '[Quote support 1]'."
2. "I also infer from Source A that [Inference 2]. This is supported by the source details: '[Quote support 2]'."
💡 Examiner Tip

Never copy the source text directly as your inference. An inference is what the source *suggests* or *implies* about the situation—reading between the lines.

Question 2: Causation Essay (12 Marks)
20 Mins

The Recipe

Write three detailed PEEL paragraphs explaining different causes, followed by a brief conclusion that explains how they link together. You are given two bullet points in the prompt, but you **MUST** provide a third point of your own knowledge to score above 6 marks.

Paragraph Structure (PEEL)

• Point: "A major cause of [Topic] was [Factor 1]."
• Evidence: "Specifically, [Detail, facts, names, dates from own knowledge]..."
• Explanation: "This led to [Topic] because [Analyze how the factor created conditions for change]..."
• Link: "Therefore, [Factor 1] was crucial because it linked to [Factor 2] by..."
💡 Examiner Tip (Level 4 Secrets)

To score Level 4 (10–12 marks), show how factors *interconnect*. For example: "The legal victory of Brown v. Topeka (Factor 1) directly triggered Southern massive resistance, which in turn forced civil rights organizations to adopt direct action campaigns (Factor 2)." This shows structured, analytical thinking.

Question 3a: Source Utility (8 Marks)
15 Mins

The Recipe

Evaluate both Source B and Source C individually using **Content** (what it shows, validated by your own knowledge) and **NOP** (Nature, Origin, Purpose - who wrote it, when, why, and how that affects reliability/utility). Finish with a brief comparative conclusion.

Writing Frame (Do this for BOTH sources)

1. Content: "Source B is useful for an enquiry into [Topic] because it reveals [Details]. This is accurate because my own knowledge shows that [Insert context facts]..."
2. NOP: "The provenance of Source B adds to its utility. Since it is a [Nature] created by [Who] in [When], its purpose is to [Why]. This makes it highly useful because it offers an authentic, contemporary perspective of..."
3. Limitations: "However, its usefulness is slightly limited because..."
💡 Examiner Tip

Never dismiss a source as "useless because it is biased". Biased sources are *highly* useful to historians because they provide direct evidence of the opinions, emotions, or propaganda of a group during that era.

Question 3b: Interpretation Difference (4 Marks)
5 Mins

The Recipe

Identify one core difference between the claims made in Interpretation 1 and Interpretation 2. Support your point with brief quotes or details from both text passages.

Writing Frame

"The main difference between the views is that Interpretation 1 suggests that [View 1], whereas Interpretation 2 suggests that [View 2]. For example, Interpretation 1 claims '[Quote 1]'. In contrast, Interpretation 2 states '[Quote 2]'."
💡 Examiner Tip

Keep it strictly focused on *what* claims are made in the texts. Do not waste time comparing the *authors*, the *years* they wrote, or their *reliability* here—that will earn 0 marks on this question.

Question 3c: Reason for Disagreement (4 Marks)
5 Mins

The Recipe

Explain one reason *why* the two historians have different viewpoints. The standard and most reliable reason is that they have relied on different primary sources as evidence.

Writing Frame

"The interpretations give different views because the historians may have relied on different primary sources as evidence. For example, Interpretation 1 relies on details like those in Source B which shows [Detail B]. In contrast, Interpretation 2 relies on details like those in Source C which shows [Detail C]."
💡 Examiner Tip

This question is a matching puzzle. Match Source B to one interpretation (which supports similar ideas), and Source C to the other interpretation. State this matchup clearly to secure full marks.

Question 3d: Interpretation Essay (16 + 4 Marks)
35 Mins

The Recipe

Evaluate how far you agree with Interpretation X's view. You must present a balanced argument using PEEL paragraphs: support Interpretation X (using details from the text, matching Source C, and own knowledge), counter with Interpretation Y (using details from the text, matching Source B, and own knowledge), and conclude with a clear, justified judgment.

Essay Structure Plan

• Intro: Introduce the debate between Interpretation 1 and 2, stating your initial thesis.
• Para 1 (Agree): Explain why Interpretation X is convincing. Reference its arguments and Source C. Back it up with detailed own knowledge facts.
• Para 2 (Counter): Explain why Interpretation Y is also convincing or presents an important alternative perspective. Reference its arguments and Source B. Back it up with detailed own knowledge facts.
• Conclusion: State exactly how far you agree with Interpretation X. Explain why its argument is stronger, or how both interpretations represent different sides of the same topic.
💡 Examiner Tip (Level 4 Secrets)

To score 13–16 marks, you must evaluate the interpretations *as interpretations*. That means explaining *why* they argue what they argue, and showing that you understand historians look at events from different angles (e.g., focusing on political legislation vs. grassroots activism).
