Name the philosophical movement that heavily influenced the expansion of Elizabethan education.|Humanism. Humanists believed that education was essential to fulfil human potential and create a civilised society, not just for training priests.
What type of school was founded for the sons of the 'middling sort' (gentry and wealthy merchants)?|Grammar schools. During Elizabeth's reign, 72 new fee-paying grammar schools were opened across the country.
What two classical subjects were heavily focused on in grammar schools?|Latin and Greek. These were considered the language of the educated elite and were essential for anyone wanting to study law or medicine.
What were 'Petty Schools'?|Small, local schools that taught basic reading, writing, and arithmetic to younger boys before they went to grammar school or an apprenticeship.
What were 'Dame Schools'?|Very basic schools run by local women in their homes, usually providing a minimal education for girls.
How were the children of the wealthy nobility educated?|By private tutors in their own homes. They learned foreign languages, history, philosophy, and social skills like fencing (for boys) or needlework (for girls).
Name the only two universities in Elizabethan England.|Oxford and Cambridge. They expanded during this period to train the clergy and professional classes in subjects like geometry, astronomy, and rhetoric.
What was the approximate male literacy rate by the end of Elizabeth's reign?|Around 30%. This significant improvement was driven by the new grammar schools and the practical need for merchants to read contracts.
What was the approximate female literacy rate by the end of the period?|Around 10%. Female literacy remained very low because society believed girls did not need a formal education and should focus on domestic skills.
How did the Protestant religion encourage the growth of education?|Protestants believed everyone should be able to read the Bible in English for themselves, motivating parishes to teach basic literacy.
Name a sport played almost exclusively by the wealthy nobility.|Real tennis (or hawking/fencing). These sports required expensive equipment, horses, or purpose-built indoor courts that only the rich could afford.
What was 'mob football'?|A highly violent, rule-less game played by the lower classes. It often involved hundreds of men from rival villages fighting to push a ball into the opponent's area.
Name two popular 'cruel sports' watched by all social classes.|Bear-baiting and cock-fighting. People from all backgrounds would mix to watch these violent animal fights and bet large sums of money on the outcome.
What was a 'bear garden'?|A purpose-built arena in London designed specifically for crowds to watch bear-baiting.
What change occurred in the type of plays being performed during Elizabeth's reign?|Traditional Catholic 'mystery plays' were replaced by secular (non-religious) plays, such as comedies and tragedies.
Name one of the first purpose-built theatres constructed outside London's city walls.|The Theatre, The Curtain, The Rose, or The Globe.
How much did the poor pay to stand in the 'pit' at the theatre?|One penny. These audience members, known as 'groundlings', made the theatre an incredibly popular and accessible form of mass entertainment.
Where did the wealthy sit in the theatre?|In the covered galleries. They paid more money for comfortable seating, protection from the weather, and to show off their high social status.
Why did acting troupes need the patronage of wealthy nobles?|To avoid being arrested as vagabonds. By being sponsored by a noble (like Leicester’s Men or The Queen’s Men), actors were legally protected as servants.
Which strict religious group heavily opposed the theatre?|The Puritans. They believed theatres were the work of the devil, encouraged sinful behaviour, and distracted people from prayer.
What happened to the population of England during Elizabeth's reign?|It grew rapidly from 3 million in 1551 to 4.2 million by 1601. This demographic boom created massive pressure on food and jobs.
How did this rapid population growth affect wages?|It drove wages down. Because there was a massive surplus of available workers, competition for jobs was fierce, allowing employers to pay less.
What agricultural practice involved fencing off traditional 'common land'?|Enclosure. This denied ordinary peasants their traditional right to graze their animals or forage for firewood, often resulting in eviction.
Why did many landowners switch from growing crops to farming sheep?|The international wool trade was booming. Woollen cloth accounted for 81.6% of England's exports, making sheep farming highly profitable.
How did the shift to sheep farming directly cause rural unemployment?|Sheep farming required significantly less physical labour than crop farming. This meant thousands of farm labourers were suddenly fired and left homeless.
What economic problem severely reduced the purchasing power of ordinary people?|Inflation. The cost of living surged, worsened by Henry VIII previously debasing the coinage (reducing its silver content).
What natural disasters caused food prices to skyrocket to starvation levels?|A series of disastrous bad harvests, particularly in 1573 and 1586.
What traditional safety net for the poor had Henry VIII destroyed in the 1530s?|The monasteries. Before their dissolution, monks had historically provided charity, food, and shelter for the sick and unemployed.
Who were the 'impotent' (or deserving) poor?|People who were genuinely unable to work due to age, illness, or disability. Elizabethan society believed it was their Christian duty to help this group.
Who were the 'idle' (or able-bodied) poor?|People who were physically capable of working but had no job. They were viewed with deep suspicion and treated as lazy criminals.
What was a 'vagabond' (or vagrant)?|A homeless, unemployed person who roamed the countryside looking for work or begging.
Give two reasons why the Elizabethan government feared vagabonds.|They believed vagabonds spread diseases (like the plague), committed crimes, and had the potential to start dangerous rebellions.
What penalty was introduced for local officials who failed to organise poor relief under the 1563 Statute of Artificers?|A fine of up to £20. This showed the government was starting to force local authorities to take the poverty crisis seriously.
What harsh physical punishments did the 1572 Vagabonds Act introduce for the 'idle poor'?|Vagrants caught begging were to be whipped and have a hole drilled through each ear as a permanent deterrent.
Despite its brutality, what helpful system did the 1572 Vagabonds Act establish?|A national poor rate. This was a local tax specifically collected to help support the 'impotent' (deserving) poor in the parish.
What was the role of the local 'Overseer of the Poor'?|To collect the poor rate from residents and distribute the money to the most vulnerable people in the community.
What was the punishment for a citizen who refused to pay the poor rate?|They could be sent to prison. The government made poor relief a mandatory civic duty rather than optional charity.
What were Justices of the Peace (JPs) required to do under the 1576 Poor Relief Act?|Provide raw materials (like wool or hemp) for the able-bodied poor to make things to sell. This helped the unemployed earn a living.
What was a 'House of Correction'?|A special prison established by the 1576 Act. Able-bodied poor who refused the offer of work were sent here to be punished.
How did government attitudes towards poverty fundamentally change between 1558 and 1588?|They shifted from purely punishing beggars to recognising that unemployment was a genuine economic problem that required government assistance.
Why did English merchants desperately need to find new markets in the 1560s?|The traditional and highly profitable wool market in Antwerp had been disrupted by the conflict with Spain.
Name two highly demanded luxury goods that merchants sought in Asia and the Americas.|Spices, silk, sugar, pearls, or tobacco. Finding direct trade routes to these goods would make merchants incredibly rich.
Who established the brutal 'triangular trade' route?|John Hawkins. He took iron goods to Africa to buy enslaved people, sold them in the New World, and brought sugar and pearls back to England.
What new type of ship was developed by the English to make ocean voyages safer?|The galleon. These ships were larger, much more stable in heavy seas, and carried heavy cannons for defence against pirates.
What specific type of sail made galleons much easier to maneuver?|Triangular (lateen) sails. This allowed the ships to catch the wind more efficiently and sail faster.
Name two new navigational instruments that allowed sailors to calculate their position.|The astrolabe and the quadrant. These allowed captains to accurately determine their latitude using the position of the stars.
What invention allowed accurate navigational books to be widely produced?|The printing press. This meant details of safe trade routes and geography could be shared among English sailors.
What vital map, created in 1569, helped sailors navigate across open oceans?|The Mercator Map. It used parallel lines of longitude and latitude to give sailors a highly realistic, standardized picture of the world.
How were incredibly expensive exploratory voyages funded?|Through private investment. Wealthy nobles and merchants (and often the Queen) bought shares in a voyage, hoping to receive a huge cut of the profits.
Why did English privateers specifically want to explore the New World?|Because Spain controlled the region. Privateers wanted to establish bases to illegally plunder Spanish treasure fleets for massive financial gain.
In what years did Sir Francis Drake complete his circumnavigation?|1577–1580. He became the first Englishman to successfully sail completely around the globe.
Did Drake originally set out with the intention of sailing around the world?|No. His primary motivation was to raid Spanish colonies on the Pacific coast and seek revenge for the Spanish attack at San Juan de Ulúa.
What was the name of Drake's only surviving ship?|The Golden Hind. He set off with five ships, but only one survived the perilous three-year journey.
What territory in North America did Drake claim for Queen Elizabeth in 1579?|Nova Albion (modern-day California). He was warmly welcomed by the local indigenous people and claimed the land for the English Crown.
Why was Drake's claim of Nova Albion highly significant politically?|It directly challenged the Pope's decree. The Pope had declared that all of the Americas belonged exclusively to Spain, which England now ignored.
Roughly how much stolen Spanish treasure did Drake return to England with?|£400,000. This astonishing haul of gold and silver was enough to pay off the entire English national debt.
What percentage profit did Drake's private investors make from the voyage?|Around 4700%. This incredible financial success inspired a massive boom in private investment for future English exploration.
How did Elizabeth reward Drake upon his return in 1580?|She publicly knighted him on the deck of the Golden Hind.
Why was Drake's knighting highly significant for Anglo-Spanish relations?|It was the ultimate insult to King Philip II. It proved that Elizabeth openly supported and rewarded the theft of Spanish gold.
How did the circumnavigation impact England's international reputation?|It proved England was a major global seafaring power capable of surviving the oceans and directly challenging the Spanish Empire.
Who was the royal favourite given the charter to colonise Virginia in 1584?|Sir Walter Raleigh. He was a famous courtier and explorer who drove the Virginia project forward.
Did Raleigh personally lead the colonists to Virginia?|No. Queen Elizabeth forbade him from leaving her side at court, so he had to organise it from England.
What was Raleigh's main role in the Virginia project?|He investigated the territory, raised the massive private funds needed, and promoted the voyage to wealthy investors.
What strategic military reason did England have for wanting a colony in North America?|To establish a safe, deep-water port. This would provide a secure base for English privateers to launch raids on Spanish treasure ships in the Caribbean.
What economic commodities did the English hope to acquire from Virginia?|Tobacco, sugar cane, gold, and animal skins. They hoped to barter English cloth for these valuable goods.
Why was establishing trade in Virginia politically important for the English economy?|It would make England less dependent on hostile European countries. By having their own colony, they wouldn't have to rely on Spain or France for luxury goods.
Who were Manteo and Wanchese?|Two Native Americans brought back to England by a fact-finding mission in 1584.
How did Thomas Harriot contribute to the planning of the colony?|He worked closely with Manteo and Wanchese to create an English-Algonquian dictionary, which would help the colonists communicate and trade.
How did Raleigh use Manteo and Wanchese to persuade investors to fund the colony?|He used them as living proof that the local indigenous people were friendly, welcoming, and open to establishing a profitable trade network.
What was the name of the specific island where the colonists attempted to settle?|Roanoke Island.
In what year did the first group of 107 colonists set sail for Virginia?|1585. The expedition was led by the naval commander Richard Grenville.
Why was the timing of their departure a major reason for the colony's failure?|They left England too late in the year. This meant they arrived too late to plant their own crops to survive the upcoming winter.
What was the name of the main supply ship that was damaged in a storm?|The Tiger.
What vital supplies were ruined when seawater breached the Tiger?|The vast majority of their perishable food, gunpowder, and the seeds required for planting crops. This disaster caused immediate starvation.
What was the main flaw in the 'mix of people' who travelled on the 1585 expedition?|They were mostly soldiers and wealthy gentlemen seeking gold. They critically lacked the physical farming skills and willingness to do manual labour.
Who was Ralph Lane?|The governor of the colony. He was a battle-hardened soldier whose aggressive, military-style leadership alienated the local Native Americans.
Why did the local Algonquian chief, Wingina, turn hostile towards the English?|He grew tired of the colonists' constant, aggressive demands for food handouts and was terrified by the lethal European diseases they brought with them.
What ultimately happened to Chief Wingina?|He planned an attack on the colony but was discovered and killed by the English, leading to open warfare.
What major European conflict prevented supply ships from rescuing the starving colonists?|The outbreak of war with Spain in 1585. Elizabeth ordered all English ships to remain at home to defend against the impending Spanish Armada.
How did the surviving 1585 colonists finally get back to England?|Facing starvation and hostile attacks, they abandoned the colony in 1586 and hitched a ride home with Sir Francis Drake, who was passing by after raiding the Caribbean.
