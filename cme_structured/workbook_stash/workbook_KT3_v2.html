Key Vocabulary
  • Shuttle Diplomacy: Kissinger's flights between Middle East capitals to negotiate disengagement agreements.
  • Knesset Visit: Sadat's historic 1977 address to the Israeli parliament in Jerusalem.
  • Menachem Begin: Israeli Prime Minister who signed the Camp David Accords and the peace treaty.
  • Jimmy Carter: US President who mediated the secret 13-day talks at Camp David in 1978.
  • Camp David Accords: The 1978 frameworks for Egypt-Israel peace and West Bank autonomy.
  • Treaty of Washington: Formal 1979 peace treaty signed by Egypt and Israel at the White House.
  • Sinai Withdrawal: Israeli military and civilian evacuation of the Sinai Peninsula, completed in 1982.
  • Arab League: Organization of Arab states which expelled Egypt in 1979 for signing the peace treaty.
  • Sadat Assassination: 1981 assassination of Anwar Sadat by military officers opposed to peace with Israel.
  • Disengagement: The physical separation of military forces after a conflict, brokered by Kissinger.
  • Autonomy: Proposed self-government for Palestinians in the West Bank/Gaza, left unresolved.
  • Suez Canal reopen: Waterway reopened in June 1975 following troop pullbacks from canal banks.

During the 1973 Yom Kippur War, Arab oil-producing nations (OAPEC) deployed the 'oil weapon' to punish the USA and its allies for supporting Israel. They severely reduced oil production and placed a complete oil embargo on the USA. This triggered a massive global economic crisis, quadrupling the price of oil. The Oil Crisis proved to the USA that the Arab-Israeli conflict was a severe threat to Western economies, forcing the USA to actively seek a peace settlement.

US Secretary of State Henry Kissinger began intense 'shuttle diplomacy', flying back and forth between Middle Eastern capitals because Arab and Israeli leaders refused to meet face-to-face. Kissinger's efforts resulted in the Sinai Disengagement Agreements, which separated the armies and successfully allowed the vital Suez Canal to reopen in 1975.

A historic breakthrough occurred in November 1977 when Egyptian President Anwar Sadat stunned the world by flying to Jerusalem and speaking at the Israeli parliament (the Knesset), offering peace in exchange for the return of the Sinai. The following month, hardline Israeli Prime Minister Menachem Begin made a reciprocal visit to Egypt. However, negotiations soon stalled over the details of Palestinian autonomy.

Determined to secure a peace deal, US President Jimmy Carter invited Sadat and Begin to Camp David, the secluded US presidential retreat, in September 1978. For 13 intense days, Carter personally mediated between the two leaders, resulting in the Camp David Accords. This framework led directly to the 1979 Treaty of Washington.

The Treaty of Washington was monumental: it was the first ever peace treaty between an Arab nation and Israel. Israel agreed to completely withdraw from the Sinai Peninsula, and in return, Egypt officially recognised Israel's right to exist and guaranteed free shipping through the Suez Canal. However, the treaty was condemned by the rest of the Arab world as a betrayal of the Palestinians, leading to Sadat's assassination by Islamic extremists in 1981.

Key Vocabulary
  • Litani Operation: 1978 Israeli military invasion of southern Lebanon to push back PLO bases.
  • Peace for Galilee: Ariel Sharon's 1982 full-scale invasion of Lebanon to destroy the PLO.
  • Beirut Siege: Israeli military blockade of West Beirut in 1982, forcing PLO exile to Tunis.
  • Sabra and Shatila: Refugee camps where Christian Phalangists massacred hundreds of Palestinians under Israeli watch.
  • Ariel Sharon: Israeli Defense Minister forced to resign after the Kahan Commission inquiry.
  • Hezbollah: Shiite Islamist militant group formed in Lebanon to resist the Israeli occupation.
  • Intifada: Arabic for 'uprising'; the grassroots Palestinian rebellion that began in December 1987.
  • Hamas: Islamist militant organization founded in Gaza in 1987, opposing Israel and competing with PLO.
  • Iron Fist: Rabin's military policy of using physical force and arrests to suppress the Intifada.
  • Kahan Commission: Israeli inquiry that found government officials indirectly responsible for Sabra and Shatila.
  • Tunis: Capital of Tunisia where the PLO relocated its headquarters in exile after being expelled from Beirut.
  • Stones Campaign: The popular image of the Intifada, showing youth throwing stones at IDF armored vehicles.

In 1974, Yasser Arafat made a historic address to the UN General Assembly, carrying a gun holster and an olive branch, famously stating: 'Do not let the olive branch fall from my hand.' This signaled the PLO's growing international recognition, as the UN granted the PLO observer status, acknowledging them as the sole legitimate representative of the Palestinian people.

After being expelled from Jordan in 1970, the PLO relocated to southern Lebanon. From this base (nicknamed 'Fatahland'), the PLO launched relentless rocket attacks and cross-border terrorist raids into northern Israel. Israel responded with fierce aerial bombardments. Seeking to permanently destroy the PLO and secure its northern border, Israel launched a massive invasion of Lebanon in 1982 (Operation Peace for Galilee).

Israeli forces advanced rapidly, besieging the Lebanese capital, Beirut. The invasion was a military success, forcing Arafat and the PLO leadership to flee into exile in Tunisia. However, it turned into a public relations disaster for Israel. Israeli forces allowed allied Lebanese Christian militias into the Sabra and Shatila refugee camps to root out hidden terrorists. Instead, the militias massacred up to 3,000 unarmed Palestinian civilians while the Israeli army stood by. The Sabra and Shatila massacre sparked global outrage and massive anti-war protests within Israel itself.

Meanwhile, conditions for Palestinians living under Israeli military occupation in the West Bank and Gaza Strip were deteriorating. They faced strict curfews, checkpoints, military arrests, and the steady expansion of illegal Jewish settlements on their land. In December 1987, decades of frustration exploded into the First Intifada (Uprising).

Unlike previous PLO military campaigns, the Intifada was a grassroots rebellion led by youths throwing stones and Molotov cocktails at heavily armed Israeli tanks. Israel responded with a harsh 'Iron Fist' policy, using live ammunition and mass arrests. The iconic television footage of heavily armed soldiers shooting at rock-throwing children severely damaged Israel's international reputation and generated massive global sympathy for the Palestinians, proving that the status quo of military occupation was unsustainable.

Key Vocabulary
  • Madrid Conference: 1991 peace summit organized by US/USSR, bringing Israel and Arab states together for the first time.
  • Gulf War: 1991 conflict where Arafat's support for Iraq isolated the PLO and cut off Gulf aid.
  • Oslo I: The 1993 Declaration of Principles establishing Palestinian self-rule frameworks.
  • Declaration of Principles: Oslo framework where PLO recognized Israel and Israel recognized PLO.
  • Yasser Arafat: PLO Chairman who renounced terrorism and became head of the Palestinian Authority.
  • Yitzhak Rabin: Israeli Prime Minister assassinated in 1995 by a Jewish extremist opposed to Oslo.
  • Palestinian Authority: The self-governing body established in 1994 to rule Gaza and West Bank cities.
  • Israel-Jordan Treaty: Historic 1994 peace treaty ending the state of war between Israel and Jordan.
  • Oslo II: 1995 agreement dividing the West Bank into Areas A, B, and C.
  • Areas A, B, and C: Security zones dividing West Bank authority between Israel and the PNA.
  • Yigal Amir: Right-wing Jewish extremist who assassinated Yitzhak Rabin in November 1995.
  • Bill Clinton: US President who hosted the historic Rabin-Arafat handshake in September 1993.

By the late 1980s, the political landscape was shifting. In 1988, Yasser Arafat made a major concession: he officially recognised Israel's right to exist (accepting UN Resolution 242) and publicly renounced terrorism. This historic compromise met the preconditions set by the USA, allowing official diplomatic dialogue to begin between the USA and the PLO.

Global geopolitics also radically changed. The end of the Cold War in 1991 meant Arab states lost their Soviet financial and military backing, leaving the USA as the sole superpower capable of brokering peace. Furthermore, during the 1991 Gulf War, Arafat made the disastrous mistake of supporting Saddam Hussein's invasion of Kuwait. In retaliation, wealthy Arab states cut off all funding to the PLO, leaving Arafat isolated and bankrupt. Both the PLO and Israel (exhausted by the Intifada) realised that a military solution was impossible.

Following secret negotiations in Norway, Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin and Yasser Arafat signed the historic Oslo Accords on the White House lawn in 1993. The Accords established mutual recognition: the PLO recognised Israel, and Israel recognised the PLO as the representative of the Palestinians.

The Oslo Accords established the Palestinian National Authority (PNA), promising a gradual handover of power. Israeli troops would withdraw from Jericho and parts of the Gaza Strip, allowing Palestinians to govern themselves for the first time. The momentum of Oslo also led to the 1994 Israel-Jordan Peace Treaty, normalizing relations between the two neighbours.

In 1995, the Oslo II agreement expanded Palestinian self-rule to major cities in the West Bank, dividing the territory into Areas A, B, and C. However, extremist factions on both sides fiercely opposed the peace process. Hamas launched suicide bombings to derail the agreements, while right-wing Israelis viewed giving up 'biblical' land as treason. Tragically, in November 1995, Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin was assassinated by a Jewish extremist, severely wounding the peace process he had championed.

🎓 Scholarly Perspective: The Flaws of Oslo Historians argue that the Oslo Accords were fundamentally flawed because they deliberately postponed the hardest issues—the final borders, the status of Jerusalem, the right of return for refugees, and the removal of Israeli settlements. By leaving these 'final status' issues unresolved, the Accords created a fragile interim agreement that extremists on both sides were easily able to sabotage.
