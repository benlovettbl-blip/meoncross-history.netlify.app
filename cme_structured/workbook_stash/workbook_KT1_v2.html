Key Vocabulary
  • Zionism: Movement advocating for an independent Jewish homeland in Palestine.
  • Certificates: Official entry permits issued by mandate authorities.
  • Insurgency: Coordinated armed rebellion against a ruling state power.
  • Mandate: League of Nations authorization for Britain to govern the territory.
  • King David Hotel: British military administrative headquarters bombed in July 1946 (91 deaths).
  • Sergeants Affair: Retaliatory hanging of two British sergeants by Irgun, breaking UK domestic resolve.
  • SS Exodus: Refugee ship turned away by Britain in 1947, provoking global outcry.
  • Resolution 181: The 1947 United Nations partition framework.
  • Deir Yassin: Palestinian village attacked by Zionist paramilitaries in April 1948, triggering widespread panic.
  • Sovereignty: Independent authority and supreme self-governing power.
  • Bevin Quota: The strict monthly limit of 1,500 Jewish immigrants to protect relations with Arab oil nations.
  • Nakba: 'Catastrophe'; the displacement of 700,000 Palestinians.

After the Second World War and the horrors of the Holocaust, the British Mandate became impossible to govern. Britain was caught in an impossible balancing act. Zionist leaders urgently demanded the creation of a Jewish state in Palestine to house European refugees. The USA strongly supported this, with President Harry Truman pressuring Britain to immediately allow 100,000 Jewish refugees into Palestine.

However, Palestinian Arabs, who formed the majority of the population, violently opposed this, demanding immediate independence and an end to mass Jewish immigration. Caught in the middle, British Foreign Secretary Ernest Bevin restricted Jewish immigration to a strict quota of just 1,500 people a month, fearing an Arab revolt that might threaten Britain's access to vital Middle Eastern oil. This alienated both sides, causing Jewish paramilitary groups to launch a violent insurgency.

Extremist Jewish groups like the Irgun and Lehi waged a violent terrorist campaign against British forces. On 22 July 1946, the Irgun blew up the southern wing of the King David Hotel in Jerusalem, housing the British military headquarters, killing 91 people. Exhausted by the violence and the immense financial cost of maintaining 100,000 troops, Britain referred the Mandate to the newly formed United Nations in February 1947.

On 29 November 1947, the UN passed Resolution 181, recommending dividing Palestine into two separate states (the Partition Plan). The Jewish state was awarded 55% of the territory. While Jewish leaders reluctantly accepted the plan, the Arab leadership completely rejected it. The UN's attempt to peacefully partition the land failed, sparking an immediate civil war.

As the British Mandate officially expired on 14 May 1948, David Ben-Gurion declared the creation of the State of Israel. The very next day, five Arab armies (from Egypt, Transjordan, Syria, Lebanon, and Iraq) launched a full-scale invasion. Despite initial Arab advances, the newly formed Israeli Defence Forces (IDF) halted the offensive. During a UN-brokered truce, Israel imported massive quantities of modern heavy weapons from Czechoslovakia. When fighting resumed, the reinforced IDF launched devastating counter-offensives, securing a decisive military victory by 1949.

🎓 Scholarly Perspective: The Warning Controversy Historians debate whether the British authorities received adequate warning of the King David Hotel bombing. The Irgun claimed they telephoned three warning calls 25 minutes prior to the blast. British officials denied receiving direct warning or dismissed it as a hoax, which resulted in the high casualty count of 91 deaths.

Key Vocabulary
  • Green Line: The 1949 armistice borders separating Israel from its Arab neighbors.
  • Nakba: Arabic for 'Catastrophe'; the flight and expulsion of 700,000 Palestinians.
  • UNRWA: UN agency established in 1949 to provide food, health care, and schooling to Palestinian refugees.
  • Law of Return: 1950 Israeli law granting automatic citizenship to any Jewish immigrant.
  • Tzena: Strict economic austerity and food rationing system implemented in early 1950s Israel.
  • Conscription: Compulsory military service introduced in Israel to maintain a massive reserve force.
  • Fedayeen: Palestinian guerrilla fighters launching cross-border raids into Israel.
  • Reprisals: Disproportionately harsh military counter-attacks launched by the IDF.
  • Suez Canal: Waterway blockaded by Egypt to choke Israeli maritime commerce.
  • Straits of Tiran: Narrow sea passage blockaded by Egypt to close off Israel's port of Eilat.
  • Count Bernadotte: UN mediator assassinated in September 1948 by the Zionist Stern Gang.
  • Right of Return: The right claimed by Palestinian refugees to return to their original homes, denied by Israel.

The 1948-49 Arab-Israeli War completely transformed the territorial map of the Middle East. As a result of the war, the proposed independent Arab state was wiped off the map. Israel captured 50% more land than the UN originally allocated, gaining control of the fertile Galilee region, the Negev Desert, and West Jerusalem.

Meanwhile, Transjordan occupied the West Bank and East Jerusalem, officially annexing it in 1951. Egypt took military control of the Gaza Strip. The new borders were defined by the 1949 Armistice Agreements and became known as the 'Green Line'. These borders left Israel's coastal plain only 9 miles wide at its narrowest point, making border defense a dominant political priority.

The war was an absolute disaster for Palestinian Arabs, who refer to the events of 1948 as the Nakba ('The Catastrophe'). Over 700,000 Palestinians fled or were forcibly expelled from their homes in the territory that became Israel. They lost their land and livelihoods, ending up in squalid, overcrowded refugee camps in the Gaza Strip, West Bank, Lebanon, Syria, and Jordan, becoming completely dependent on UN aid.

To secure the new state, David Ben-Gurion passed the Law of Return in 1950, granting any Jew in the world the right to immigrate to Israel and receive immediate citizenship. This caused the population to double within three years. He also introduced universal conscription, cementing the newly formed Israeli Defence Forces (IDF) as the backbone of Israeli society and national identity.

To prevent economic collapse from this massive demographic growth, the Israeli government secured vital financial aid from the USA, cementing a crucial geopolitical alliance. Following the war, relations between Israel and Egypt were characterized by deep hostility. Egypt bitterly resented its military defeat and the massive burden of 200,000 Palestinian refugees now trapped in the Gaza Strip.

Source C: Account of the Nakba
"We left everything behind—our homes, our orchards, our entire history. We thought we would return in a few weeks when the fighting stopped, but we became permanent exiles in tents."
⚖️ Dual Interpretation: The Competing Narratives of 1948
Traditional Israeli Narrative
This perspective emphasises that the 1948 conflict was a miraculous 'War of Independence' where the infant Jewish state overcame overwhelming odds against five invading Arab armies to ensure a safe haven following the Holocaust. The refugee crisis is often viewed as a consequence of Arab leaders urging civilians to flee.
 Palestinian and 'New Historian' Narrative
This perspective points out that the UN Partition Plan was inherently unjust. Rather than a simple defensive war, revisionist historians argue Israeli forces carried out deliberate forced expulsions (Plan Dalet). For Palestinians, 1948 is the Nakba, where 700,000 civilians were driven from their homes.

Key Vocabulary
  • Pan-Arabism: Ideology promoting the unification of Arab nations under common political leadership.
  • Czech Arms Deal: 1955 agreement providing Egypt with advanced Soviet jet fighters and tanks.
  • Aswan Dam: Massive Egyptian infrastructure project which lost Western funding, prompting canal seizure.
  • Nationalisation: The act of bringing private assets (the Suez Canal) under government ownership.
  • Protocol of Sèvres: Secret agreement outlining the military collusion between Britain, France, and Israel.
  • Sinai Peninsula: Vast desert territory captured by Israel during the swift October campaign.
  • Port Said: Egyptian canal city where British and French paratroopers landed.
  • UNEF: First UN peacekeeping force, deployed in Sinai to separate Egyptian and Israeli forces.
  • UAR: The 1958 political union between Egypt and Syria, representing the height of Pan-Arabism.
  • Eisenhower: US President who forced the withdrawal of invading forces using financial leverage.
  • Austerity: Severe economic cuts, experienced in Egypt due to dam expenses and invasion damages.
  • Pan-Arab hero: Nasser's status after surviving the invasion and retaining nationalised control of the canal.

In 1952, a group of 'Free Officers' overthrew the Egyptian monarchy. By 1954, Colonel Gamal Abdel Nasser emerged as the leader of Egypt. Nasser became a hero across the Middle East by promoting Pan-Arabism—the idea that all Arab nations should unite to resist Western imperialism and oppose Israel. He positioned Egypt as the undisputed leader of the Arab world.

Tensions with Israel escalated due to frequent cross-border raids by Palestinian 'Fedayeen' (freedom fighters/terrorists) operating from Egyptian-controlled Gaza. In February 1955, following a Fedayeen attack, the IDF launched a massive, disproportionate reprisal raid on an Egyptian army headquarters in Gaza, killing 38 Egyptian soldiers. This humiliated Nasser, proving his army was weak, and pushed him to sign the Czech Arms Deal (1955) to acquire Soviet-bloc weapons.

In July 1956, the USA and Britain abruptly cancelled their funding for Nasser's flagship infrastructure project, the Aswan High Dam. In furious retaliation, Nasser nationalised the Suez Canal, seizing it from its British and French owners to use its toll revenues to build the dam. This outraged Britain and France, who viewed Nasser as a threat to their global empires.

Britain, France, and Israel held a secret meeting and agreed to the Protocol of Sèvres. Their plan was for Israel to invade the Egyptian Sinai Peninsula. Britain and France would then intervene under the false pretense of 'protecting' the canal, allowing them to seize it back and overthrow Nasser. On 29 October 1956, Israel invaded the Sinai, easily defeating the Egyptian forces.

However, the plot was a massive geopolitical miscalculation. US President Eisenhower was furious at the deception and threatened to ruin the British economy unless they withdrew. The UN condemned the invasion, forcing a humiliating British and French retreat. Despite losing the military battle, Nasser emerged as a political hero who had successfully defied Western empires, leading to the brief formation of the United Arab Republic (UAR) with Syria in 1958.

Source D: Anthony Eden's Broadcast (1956)
"British Prime Minister Anthony Eden stated: 'We have stepped in to separate the combatants and to safeguard the Canal... If Nasser is allowed to get away with this act of plunder, the whole Middle East will fall into chaos.'"
