Key Vocabulary
  • Cairo Conference: 1964 Arab League summit that founded the PLO and initiated water diversion plans.
  • PLO: Palestine Liberation Organization, founded to coordinate national resistance.
  • Jordan River: Waterway whose headwaters were contested in the border water wars.
  • Samu Raid: Devastating 1966 Israeli reprisal raid in the Jordanian-controlled West Bank.
  • MiG fighters: Soviet jet fighters flown by Syria, six of which were shot down in April 1967.
  • Straits of Tiran: Egyptian waterway closed by Nasser in May 1967, blockading Eilat port.
  • Operation Focus: Israel's preemptive airstrike on 5 June 1967 that destroyed Arab air capabilities.
  • Sinai Peninsula: Vast Egyptian desert territory captured by Israel.
  • Gaza Strip: Densely populated coastal strip captured from Egypt.
  • West Bank: Territory captured from Jordan, placing 1 million Palestinians under military rule.
  • Golan Heights: Strategic high ground captured from Syria in northern Israel.
  • Preemptive Strike: A military strike launched to prevent an expected imminent attack by an enemy.

The seeds of the 1967 war were sown at the 1964 Cairo Conference, where Arab leaders established the Palestine Liberation Organisation (PLO). Tension escalated sharply between Israel, Syria, and Jordan over control of the Jordan River waters. Syria aggressively supported cross-border guerrilla raids into Israel by Fatah (a militant Palestinian group). In November 1966, after a landmine killed three Israeli soldiers, Israel launched a massive reprisal raid on the Jordanian village of Samu, destroying over 40 homes and humiliating King Hussein of Jordan.

Tension reached boiling point on 7 April 1967, when an artillery clash over the Syrian border escalated into an intense aerial dogfight. The Israeli Air Force shot down six Syrian MiG fighter jets and flew triumphantly over the Syrian capital, Damascus, severely humiliating the Syrian government.

In May 1967, the USSR provided Nasser with false intelligence claiming that Israel was massing troops on the Syrian border for a full-scale invasion. Feeling pressure to prove his leadership of the Arab world, Nasser engaged in dangerous brinkmanship. He ordered UN peacekeepers out of the Sinai Peninsula, moved 100,000 Egyptian troops to the Israeli border, and closed the Straits of Tiran to Israeli shipping—an act Israel considered an explicit declaration of war.

The USA, bogged down in Vietnam, urged restraint but privately acknowledged Israel's right to defend itself. Facing the threat of an imminent three-front invasion by Egypt, Syria, and Jordan, Israel decided to strike first. On the morning of 5 June 1967, Israel launched a devastating preemptive strike (Operation Focus).

In just a few hours, the Israeli Air Force completely destroyed the Egyptian, Syrian, and Jordanian air forces while they were still on the ground. With total air superiority, Israeli ground forces swept rapidly through the Sinai Peninsula, reaching the Suez Canal within three days. Similar lightning victories occurred against Jordan and Syria. The war ended after just six days in a spectacular military triumph for Israel and a crushing, humiliating defeat for the Arab states.

Source E: Gamal Abdel Nasser Speech (May 1967)
"The armed forces yesterday occupied Sharm el-Sheikh. It is an affirmation of our rights and our sovereignty over the Gulf of Aqaba. The Jews threaten war. We tell them: You are welcome, we are ready for war."

Key Vocabulary
  • UN Resolution 242: UN framework proposing 'Land for Peace' to resolve the Arab-Israeli conflict.
  • Three Nos: Khartoum Conference declaration: No peace, No recognition, No negotiations with Israel.
  • War of Attrition: Low-intensity artillery and border war between Egypt and Israel (1967–70).
  • Settlements: Israeli civilian communities established in the occupied territories.
  • PFLP: Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine, a Marxist militant faction of the PLO.
  • Dawson's Field: Airstrip in Jordan where hijacked planes were blown up by the PFLP in 1970.
  • Black September: 1970 civil war in Jordan resulting in the military expulsion of the PLO.
  • Lebanon: State where the PLO relocated its military bases and headquarters after Jordan.
  • Munich Olympics: 1972 games where Black September terrorists killed 11 Israeli athletes.
  • Wrath of God: Covert Mossad campaign to assassinate those behind the Munich massacre.
  • Yasser Arafat: Leader of Fatah who became chairman of the PLO in 1969.
  • Khartoum Conference: 1967 Arab summit formulating the unified refusal to negotiate with Israel.

The aftermath of the 1967 war drastically altered the Middle East map. In just six days, Israel tripled its size by capturing the Golan Heights (from Syria), the Gaza Strip and Sinai Peninsula (from Egypt), and the West Bank and East Jerusalem (from Jordan). These became known as the 'occupied territories'. This caused a massive new wave of Palestinian refugees and left Israel occupying land inhabited by over a million hostile Palestinians.

In November 1967, the UN passed Resolution 242, which established the principle of 'Land for Peace'. It called for Israel to withdraw from the occupied territories in exchange for Arab recognition of Israel's right to exist. However, the Arab League issued the 'Three Noes' (No peace, No recognition, No negotiation with Israel), and Israel refused to withdraw, leaving the Suez Canal closed and disputed.

The crushing defeat of the Arab armies convinced Palestinian leaders that they could no longer rely on Arab states to destroy Israel. Under Yasser Arafat's leadership, the PLO embraced international terrorism to force the world to pay attention to their cause. In 1970, a radical PLO faction called the PFLP hijacked four international passenger airplanes and blew them up at Dawson's Field in Jordan.

King Hussein of Jordan, fearing the PLO was taking over his country, declared martial law. In a brutal conflict known as 'Black September' (1970), Jordanian forces killed thousands of Palestinian fighters and expelled the PLO entirely, forcing them to relocate their headquarters to Lebanon.

In revenge, a secretive Palestinian terrorist group calling themselves 'Black September' targeted the 1972 Munich Olympic Games. They broke into the Olympic Village, taking eleven Israeli athletes hostage, demanding the release of Palestinian prisoners. Following a botched rescue attempt by the German police, all eleven Israeli athletes were murdered. This horrific event shocked the world, drawing immense international condemnation but achieving the PLO's goal of thrusting the Palestinian issue onto the global stage. Israel responded with Operation Wrath of God, a covert assassination campaign targeting the perpetrators.

🎓 Scholarly Perspective: The Impact of Munich Historians note the paradox of the Munich massacre. While it was a spectacular tactical failure that brought universal condemnation upon the Palestinian cause, it was a massive strategic success in terms of publicity. Before Munich, the world largely viewed the issue as an Arab-Israeli border dispute; after Munich, the specific plight of the Palestinians became impossible to ignore.

"Affirms that the fulfillment of Charter principles requires... Withdrawal of Israeli armed forces from territories occupied in the recent conflict; and termination of all claims or states of belligerency."

Key Vocabulary
  • Anwar Sadat: Egyptian President who planned the surprise 1973 attack to break the diplomatic deadlock.
  • Bar-Lev Line: Israel's 25-meter-high sand fortification along the Suez Canal.
  • Yom Kippur: The holiest day in Judaism, when Egypt and Syria launched their surprise invasion.
  • Water Cannons: High-pressure hose equipment used by Egypt to breach the sand walls of the Bar-Lev Line.
  • Operation Nickel Grass: US military airlift that resupplied Israel with ammunition and replacement tanks.
  • OPEC: Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries, which launched the oil embargo.
  • Oil Embargo: Ban on oil sales to Western nations, quadrupling prices and causing economic panic.
  • Resolution 338: UN Security Council resolution that called for an immediate ceasefire in October 1973.
  • Shuttle Diplomacy: Henry Kissinger's flights between Middle East capitals to negotiate troop withdrawals.
  • Golda Meir: Israeli Prime Minister who faced severe public criticism for lack of war preparedness.
  • Moshe Dayan: Israeli Defense Minister whose confidence was shattered by the initial Arab breakthroughs.
  • Soviet Expulsion: Sadat's 1972 dismissal of 15,000 Soviet advisors to show independence from Moscow.

Between 1967 and 1970, Egypt and Israel engaged in the 'War of Attrition', firing artillery across the Suez Canal in a low-level conflict that exhausted both sides but changed no borders. Following Nasser's sudden death in 1970, Anwar Sadat became President of Egypt. Sadat realised that Egypt's economy was failing and that he desperately needed to reopen the Suez Canal and recover the Sinai Peninsula.

Sadat initially attempted diplomacy, but Israel, feeling militarily invincible after 1967, refused to negotiate. Israel was busy consolidating its control over the occupied territories. They constructed the Bar Lev Line—a massive, seemingly impenetrable sand-wall fortification stretching along the Suez Canal. They also began building Jewish settlements in the captured territories to permanently cement their control.

Realising diplomacy had failed, Sadat concluded that only a massive military shock could force Israel to negotiate. In a dramatic shift in Cold War allegiances, Sadat expelled 15,000 Soviet military advisors from Egypt in 1972, hoping to win favour with the USA, which he believed was the only superpower capable of pressuring Israel.

On 6 October 1973, during the holiest day in the Jewish calendar (Yom Kippur), Egypt and Syria launched a massive, highly coordinated surprise attack. Caught completely off guard with its reserve army at home, Israel suffered catastrophic early losses. Egyptian forces used high-pressure water cannons to blast holes in the Bar Lev Line, crossing the Suez Canal, while Syrian tanks overran the Golan Heights.

The war quickly escalated into a Cold War proxy conflict. The USSR began a massive airlift of weapons to re-supply Egypt and Syria, prompting US President Richard Nixon to launch an even larger emergency airlift (Operation Nickel Grass) to save Israel. Armed with new US weapons and fully mobilised reserves, Israel launched a devastating counter-attack, pushing the Syrians back toward Damascus and trapping the Egyptian Third Army. The UN imposed a ceasefire on 25 October, ending the Yom Kippur War. While Israel won militarily, the early Arab successes shattered Israel's aura of invincibility and restored Arab pride, setting the stage for future negotiations.

"There was no doubt that we were facing a terrible threat... We had been caught by surprise, not by the fact of war, but by the timing."
